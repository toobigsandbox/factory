Geography // Work of Water

Ocean Waves — Landform Formation

Guide: Elim Lee // AMI Elementary Submission

Purpose

To observe how the constant, rhythmic motion of ocean waves erodes coastlines and creates distinct geographical landforms.

Materials

  • Wave Tray: A long, rectangular tray.
  • Sand/Clay: To build a "cliff" at one end of the tray.
  • Water: Filling half the tray.
  • Paddle: A small board or hand to create rhythmic waves.
  • Photos of Sea Caves, Arches, and Stacks.

Presentation

Inventory Note: AMI Geography Charts showing coastal features (e.g., peninsulas, bays, capes) are copyrighted. Ensure these are displayed for the children to compare with their tray experiments.
Set up the tray with a steep sandy cliff at one end and water at the other.
"We have seen how rivers flow from high to low. But there is another kind of water that never rests. The ocean is always moving against the land."
Use the paddle to create gentle, rhythmic waves moving toward the cliff.
"Watch where the water hits the land. It strikes the bottom of the cliff over and over. Every wave brings a little bit of the land back with it into the sea."
Fig 6.1: Demonstrating wave erosion on a coastal model
Show the photos of the Sea Cave and the Arch.
"If the water finds a soft spot in the rock, it carves out a hole called a cave. If it keeps carving all the way through, it becomes an arch. Eventually, the top of the arch might fall, leaving a pillar of rock standing all alone in the water—we call that a stack."
Fig 6.2: The stages of coastal erosion landforms
"Guide Tip: This is a lesson in persistence. The waves are not as strong as the rock, but they are constant. This is a perfect link to the 'Fundamental Needs' in History—how people chose where to land their ships based on these carved harbors."
Elim Lee // Semester 4 Sprint // AMI Elementary
