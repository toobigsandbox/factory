Geography // Work of Water

Rainwater Erosion & The Protection of Soil

Guide: Elim Lee // AMI Elementary Submission

Purpose

To demonstrate how falling water shapes the land and how the biological world (roots) provides a vital defense against soil loss.

Materials

  • Two Trays: One with bare sand, one with a "rooted" mound (moss or turf).
  • Water Dropper & Watering Can: To simulate light and heavy rain.
  • Cap Stone: A small pebble.
  • Catch Buckets: To observe runoff clarity.

Part I: Splash Erosion & Earth Pillars

Fill the dropper and let single drops fall from a height onto the bare sand.
"Even a single drop of rain has energy. Look at the crater it makes. This is splash erosion—the water is already moving the earth."
Place a pebble on the sand. Drip water around it.
"But look here. The sand under the stone is protected. While the rain washes away everything else, it leaves a pillar of earth standing tall. In nature, we call these Earth Pillars or Hoodoos."

Part II: The Defense of the Roots

Use the watering can to "rain" on the bare mound, then the rooted mound.
"On the bare hill, the water takes the earth away instantly. It is a flood of mud. But on the hill with roots? The water comes out clear. The roots are like millions of tiny hands holding the earth in place."
Fig 7.1: Comparison of runoff and soil stability
"Guide Tip: This lesson provides the 'Moral' of the Work of Water. Water is a cosmic agent of change, but life (the plants) acts as a cosmic agent of balance. Without roots, the mountains would be leveled much faster."
