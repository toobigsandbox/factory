Geography // Work of Water

Rainwater Erosion & Earth Pillars

Guide: Elim Lee // AMI Elementary Submission

Purpose

To observe how falling water shapes the land through erosion and to understand the formation of earth pillars.

Materials

  • 3D River Model: Specifically a flat or sloped section of dry, fine sand/soil.
  • Water Dropper (Pipette) and a small cup of water.
  • Cap Stones: Small flat pebbles or coins.
  • Photos of natural Earth Pillars (e.g., Bryce Canyon or the Dolomites).

Presentation

Inventory Note: Reference to specific AMI Geography Charts (Work of Water series) should be noted; these charts remain proprietary and are not included in the digital repository.
Gather the children around the 3D model. Point to a smooth, dry mound of sand.
"We have talked about how rivers carry water across the land. But what happens when the water first falls from the sky as rain?"
Fill the dropper. Hold it high above the sand and let single drops fall.
"Look at what happens to the sand where the drop hits. It makes a little crater. The water is actually moving the earth even before it becomes a river."
Fig 2.1: Water droplets creating impact craters in dry sand
Place a small pebble on top of a fresh section of the sand mound.
"What if something hard is protecting the sand? Let's place this 'cap stone' here and see what the rain does now."
Drip water continuously around the pebble, but not directly on it, or let it roll off the pebble.
"The rain washes away all the unprotected sand. But the sand under our stone is safe. Look at what is happening!"
Fig 2.2: Formation of an Earth Pillar (Hoodoos)
"Guide Tip: This is an impressionistic lesson. The goal is not to name every type of soil, but to witness the 'Power of Water'—one of the great cosmic agents of change. In nature, these are often called Hoodoos or Fairy Chimneys."
