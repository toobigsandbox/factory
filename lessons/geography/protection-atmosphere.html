Geography // The Sun and the Earth

Protection of the Atmosphere and the Rains

Guide: Elim Lee // AMI Elementary

Direct Aim

To understand the atmosphere's role as a protective layer and a regulator of moisture (rain).

Materials

Charts 19A-23A. Candle, matches, sand, hot plate, heatproof mat, hairdryer, glass tray, saucepan, clear glass bowl, water.

Part I: The Atmosphere as Protection

Invite the children and show Chart 19A. Use the candle to represent the intense heat of the Sun.
"Remember how we talked about the Sun’s rays? If they hit us directly without any protection, it would be far too hot for life. The Earth has a special skin called the atmosphere."
Light the candle. Hold a hand a safe distance away, then move a piece of glass or a card between the hand and the flame.
"The atmosphere acts like this shield. It reflects some of the heat back into space and absorbs others, making the Earth's temperature just right for us."
Illustration: Guide demonstrating the shield effect with a candle and a barrier.
Show Chart 20A (The Atmosphere protecting from meteorites).
"Outer space is full of rocks called meteorites traveling very fast. When they hit our 'skin'—the atmosphere—they rub against the air molecules so fast they catch fire and burn up. We see them as shooting stars."

Part II: The Rains (Experiments)

Set up the hairdryer and a glass tray with a small amount of water.
"We know the Sun heats the water on Earth. Let's see what happens to that moisture. If I use this hairdryer to act like the Sun's heat..."
Turn on the hairdryer over the tray. Observe the water disappearing.
"The water turns into invisible vapor and rises. But where does it go?"
Illustration: The hairdryer experiment showing evaporation.
Set up the saucepan with water on the hot plate. Place the large glass bowl upside down over the pot.
"Imagine the pot is the warm surface of the Earth and this bowl is our atmosphere. As the water gets hot, look at what forms on the inside of the bowl."
Observe the "fog" forming, then cooling and turning into droplets that run down the sides.
"The vapor hits the cooler 'atmosphere' and turns back into liquid. When the droplets get too heavy, they fall. This is rain."

Part III: Orographic Rain

Discuss mountains using the visual of the glass bowl.
"When warm, wet air hits a mountain, it is forced to climb. As it gets higher, the air cools down, just like when the vapor hit our cool bowl. It drops its water as rain on one side of the mountain."
Illustration: Diagram of orographic rain showing wet vs. dry sides of a mountain.
