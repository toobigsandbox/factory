Geography // Work of Water

Highlands to Lowlands & Rainfall Maps

Guide: Elim Lee // AMI Elementary Submission

Purpose

To understand how elevation and rainfall distribution dictate the path, speed, and volume of rivers.

Materials

  • Topographic (Raised Relief) Map: A map where the mountains can be felt.
  • Rainfall Map: A map showing precipitation density (See Copyright Note).
  • 3D River Model: To reference back to previous tactile work.
  • Blue yarn or thread.

Presentation

Inventory Note: Standard Rainfall Charts and Topographic Atlases are copyrighted materials. This repository contains only the procedural guide for using these physical resources.
Place the 3D model and the Topographic map side by side.
"In our model, we saw the water rushing down the steep slopes of the mountains into the flat plains. Mapmakers have a special way of showing these 'Highlands' and 'Lowlands' on flat paper."
Run your finger over the brown/red areas of the map, then the green areas.
"The brown parts show us where the earth rises high. The green parts show us the low, flat land. Where do you think a river would flow fastest?"
Fig 3.1: Abstraction of a river on a topographic map
Bring out the Rainfall Map. Overlay it or place it nearby.
"Look at these areas with the darkest blue. This is where the most rain falls. If this rain falls on the highlands, it creates the mighty rivers that travel all the way to the lowlands."
Invite the child to use a blue thread to connect a high-rainfall highland area to the nearest coast on the map.
"Can you follow the water's journey from where it falls to where it finds the sea?"
"Guide Tip: Children at this age are fascinated by 'The Most.' Look for the Amazon or the Ganges—rivers fed by massive rainfall and massive mountains. This connects the 'Work of Water' to the 'Work of Air' (the winds that bring the rain)."
