Geography // Human Interdependencies

Economic Geography

Master Module (Presentations I - VIII)

Purpose

  • To understand how and why humans are interlinked across distances.
  • To see the cause and effect of resources on civilization.
  • Practical application of arithmetic (graphs, rounding, ratios).

Materials

Outline maps (State/US/World), economic stamps, ink tray, colored pencils, graph paper, millimeter ruler, almanacs/encyclopedias.

I & II: Produced and Where / Regional Context

Place a state map. Use a cow stamp for a unit of production.
"One stamp will represent one ranch. We'll place a stamp wherever your research found a herd."
Add Citrus, Cotton, and Copper symbols to show major state produce.
"Arizona produces citrus, cotton, and even copper. Look at the group clusters of the copper mines—I wonder if something under the earth causes them to show up there?"

III & IV: National Ratios / Consumption Logic

Show a US Beef map where 1 stamp = 300,000 cows (Quantity) or 1% (Ratio).
"Perspective changes the story. This legend says one stamp equals 300,000 cows. Arizona only has half a cow—why? Because we are comparing it to the whole nation."
Ask: "How much milk does one person drink in a day? A year?"
"If one person drinks 90 gallons a year, and there are 332 million people in the U.S., how much is consumed altogether? Let's create a plan to solve this."

V & VI: Bar Graph Activity / Production vs. Consumption

Use graph paper. Define: 1 square = 10 eggs. Graph AZ (115) vs SC (130).
"For Arizona, we count by tens: 110... but we need 115. We'll draw a halfway line through the last square."
Compare US Poultry Produced (37B lbs) vs. Consumed (30B lbs).
"We produced 37 Billion and consumed 30 Billion. What do we do with the extra? We send it away—we export it."
