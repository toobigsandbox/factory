Economic Geography

Human Dependence on Natural Resources

Purpose

To present the concept of economic geography and illustrate how humans depend on, transform, and distribute natural resources across different regions.

Materials

World map, prepared state/country outline maps, resource/product cards, rubber stamps or hand-drawn symbols, ink pads, and reference atlases/almanacs.

Presentation I: The Local Industry

Gather a small group. Start a conversation based on recent research or a local feature (e.g., Ranches in Arizona).
"You found all the different ranches in our state during your research. I wonder what that would look like on a map? Would they all be in one place, or spread out along the river?"
Place an outline map of the state. Show the children how to use a symbol (or stamp) to represent one unit of production.
"We'll put a stamp wherever we found a ranch. One stamp will represent one ranch where cows are raised. Let’s see—your data said there was one right in this area."

Presentation II: Quantity vs. Perspective

Compare the state map to a national map showing beef production.
"In our first map, one stamp meant one ranch. But look at this national map. Here, one stamp equals 300,000 cows! Why does Arizona only have half a cow here? Ah, it's because this map shows the percentage of the whole country's production."
Discuss the importance of the Legend (Key) for interpreting data.
"The key is our code. It tells the reader exactly what the symbols mean so they can understand the story the map is telling."
"Broaden the child's definition of 'produce.' In economic geography, this isn't just the grocery store section—we 'produce' copper, timber, and textiles. Encourage them to see the centralization of industries near resources like rivers."
