Geography // Work of Water

River Work: Carving, Carrying, Depositing

Guide: Elim Lee // AMI Elementary Submission

Purpose

To demonstrate the three main functions of a river and explore how layers of sediment create a record of geological time.

Materials

  • Sand Tray: A large tray with a mound of sand sloped at one end.
  • Water Pitcher: Small, with a steady-pouring spout.
  • Colored Sand: Three distinct colors (e.g., Red, Yellow, Grey) to represent different eras.
  • Labels: Source, Course, Banks, Delta.

Presentation Part I: The Mechanics

Gather children around the sand tray. Pour water steadily at the top of the slope.
"Watch the sand. See how the water is digging a path? It is cutting into the earth. We call this 'Carving'. Now look at the water—it is murky. The river is acting like a truck, 'Carrying' the sand."
Observe the water as it reaches the flat bottom and drops the sand.
"As the land gets flat, the water moves slower. It can't hold the heavy sand anymore. Watch it drop the sand right here. This is called 'Depositing'."
Fig 4.1: The physical work of the river

Presentation Part II: Layers of Time

Pedagogical Note: This is Day 2 of the River Model. The goal is to connect the "Work of Water" to the Timeline of Life.
Spread a layer of red sand over the mountain slope. Pour water.
"Imagine this red sand is the soil from a time long ago. We see the river carry it and deposit it at the bottom."
Spread a layer of yellow sand on top of the red sand. Pour water.
"Now time has passed. New plants have grown and died, making a new kind of soil. The river carries this yellow soil and drops it on top of the red."
Fig 4.2: Sedimentary layers forming a chronological record
"Scientists can look at these layers—red, then yellow, then grey—and they can tell us exactly what was happening on the Earth millions of years before we were here. The river is like a librarian, filing away the history of the world in these layers."
"Guide Tip: This lesson provides a marvelous entrance into Geology and Paleontology. I wonder what kind of scientists study these layers? (Geologists!)"
