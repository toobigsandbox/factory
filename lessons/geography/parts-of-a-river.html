Geography // Work of Water

Rivers: Major Rivers & Flow Patterns

Guide: Elim Lee // AMI Elementary Submission

Purpose

To understand how rivers form, flow, and shape the land they pass through using a three-dimensional model.

Materials

  • 3D River Model: Large landscape model built previously.
  • Labeling Kit: Toothpicks and small paper flags with nomenclature.
  • Wall Map or Atlas (See Instructor's Note on Copyright).
  • Blue pencil for tracing.

Presentation

Inventory Note: Standard AMI Charts (e.g., The River Chart) and Maps are copyright protected and are not stored within this repository. Ensure these physical resources are present on the shelf for the child's reference.
Gather the children around the 3D River Model.
"You remember how we built this model to show the high lands and the low lands. Today, we are going to look at the life of a river as it moves through this landscape."
Identify the highest point of the model. Place a flag labeled 'SOURCE' on a toothpick into the model.
"A river always starts in a high place. Gravity pulls the water down. This starting point is what we call the 'Source'."
Fig 1.1: Physical labeling of the 3D Model with Toothpick Flags
Trace the flow with your finger. Invite a child to place a 'MOUTH' flag where the river meets the edge of the model (or the 'sea').
"As the water travels downstream, it finds its way to the lowest point. This end of the journey is the 'Mouth'."
Hand a child a blue pencil and a paper map.
"Now that we have labeled our own model, let's see if we can find these same patterns on our map of the world."
"Guide Tip: This lesson is the bridge between the sensorial 3D model and the abstract map. Ensure the toothpicks are placed firmly so the labels are readable at the child's eye level."
