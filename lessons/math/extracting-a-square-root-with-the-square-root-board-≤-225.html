Math // Square Roots

Extracting a Square Root with the Square Root Board (≤ 225)

Guide: Elim Lee // AMI Elementary

Purpose

To provide the child with a sensorial method for extracting the square root of any number up to 225, and to introduce the concept of a remainder in square roots.

Material Analysis

  • Square root board (15 x 15 divots).
  • Container of green unit beads (minimum 125, ideally 225).
  • Small dish or pottery bowl.
  • Felt mat.
  • Paper and pencil (graph paper optional).

Presentation

Gather a group and show the materials. Place the board on the mat.
- "Today, we're going to learn how to extract square roots using the square root board."
Set the problem: Find the square root of 14.
- "What is the square root of 14? Now, we don’t have a bead square with exactly 14 beads, but that doesn’t mean it doesn’t have a square root. First, let’s count out 14 beads into our little bowl."
Count 14 beads. Set the container aside. Place the board to the child's right.
Place one bead in the corner (1x1). Then extend by one in each direction and complete the square (2x2).
- "Great! That’s the square of two. Let’s try again—extend by one bead in each direction and complete the square."
Complete the 3x3 square.
- "Now we have the square of three. Can we build a bigger square? Let's check our bowl."
Attempt to extend to 4x4. Discover there are only 5 beads left (7 needed).
- "Hmm, no, I can’t complete the next square. So, the biggest complete square we made with 14 beads is 3 by 3. The square root of 14 is 3, with 5 leftover. That leftover is called the remainder."
Record: $\sqrt{14} = 3 \text{ r } 5$.
Repeat with 35. Count 35 beads. Build until the 5x5 square is complete.
- "The answer here is 5 with a remainder of 10."
"We want children to have plenty of sensorial experiences here. Stick with numbers like 66 or 77—numbers that seem large but are manageable. This builds excitement and repetition. Try counting out 210 beads and making a square on paper sometime, just to see how long it takes. This is why the bead board is so valuable—it makes math manageable and fun."
Elim Lee // April 1st Milestone Edition
