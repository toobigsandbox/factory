Math // Squares and Cubes

Concept and Notation of Square Root

Guide: Elim Lee // AMI Elementary

Direct Aim

To introduce the concept of the square root sensorially and to provide the historical context of its notation.

Material Analysis

Mat, squares from the bead cabinet (1-10), paper, pencil.

Presentation

Gather a small group. Invite the children to select a square and lay it out on the mat.
- "Let's look at this square of four. How many beads are in it total?"
Count the beads or look at the dimensions.
- "Sixteen beads. We know this is sixteen because it is four along the bottom and four along the side. $4 \times 4$ is sixteen. We say sixteen is the square of four."
- "When we want to find the number that grew the square—the number along the side—we call that the square root. So, what is the square root of sixteen? It is four."
Repeat with other squares, such as the 6-square (36) and the 8-square (64).

The Story of Notation

Show the radical symbol (√) on paper.
- "Long ago, mathematicians wrote the word 'radix' to mean root. Eventually, they just wrote a cursive 'r'. Because they were in a hurry and excited to do math, that 'r' started to look like this symbol we use today."
EVOLUTION FROM RADIX (r) TO RADICAL (√)
$\sqrt{16} = 4$
"René Descartes is often credited with the popularization of the modern radical sign. It's a wonderful example of how notation evolves through the practical need for speed and clarity in communication."
Elim Lee // April 1st Milestone Edition
