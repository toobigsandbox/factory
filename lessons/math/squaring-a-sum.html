Subject Area: Mathematics Guide Name: Elim Lee

Squaring a Sum

Chapter III: Squares and Cubes of Numbers

Direct Aim

To provide the child with further experience building the geometric pattern of the square of a binomial and a trinomial.

Indirect Aim

To introduce the formal algebraic rules of polynomial expansion, and specifically the vertical way of writing the multiplication of a polynomial by a polynomial.

Materials

  • Felt mat (neutral backdrop)
  • Bead bars 1–9 and bead squares 1–9 of the Bead Cabinet
  • Box of number cards (white and grey) and operation signs
  • Parentheses slips
  • Blank tickets, paper, and pencil

PRESENTATION A: Squaring a Binomial

  1. Show the materials laid out neatly: box of beads, squares of numbers, felt mat, parentheses, grey and white number cards, operation signs, pencil, and paper. "Today we are going to explore squaring a sum. This lesson will help you build your own squares using any numbers you choose. We will rely on our previous work with the commutative and distributive laws of multiplication."
  2. Invite the children to construct the expression \((4 + 3) \times (4 + 3)\) using the number cards, parentheses, and operation signs. Place them in a horizontal line on the felt mat. "Let's make our expression on the mat. First, we place the open parenthesis, then the number four, a plus sign, the number three, and a closing parenthesis. Now, we place our multiplication sign, and repeat: open parenthesis, four, plus, three, close parenthesis."
  3. Explain that we will multiply each term systematically. Point to the multiplier term of \(4\), then lay out the corresponding bead bars to show \(4\) taken \(4\) times, and \(3\) taken \(4\) times. "To square the sum, we multiply each term in the first parentheses by each term in the second parentheses. The first term of our multiplier is four. First, we multiply four by four, which gives us our yellow four-square. Next, we multiply three by four—which means three taken four times. Let's place four pink three-bead bars on the mat."
  4. Move to the second multiplier term, \(3\). Lay out \(4\) taken \(3\) times, and \(3\) taken \(3\) times without removing any previously placed beads. "Now we look at our second multiplier, which is three. We multiply four by three—four taken three times. Let's place three yellow four-bead bars next to our pink ones. Finally, we multiply three by three, which gives us our pink three-square. Let's place that in the remaining corner."
  5. Count the total bead bars along each outer edge to prove a perfect square has been formed. "Look! By placing these beads, we have built a larger perfect square. Let's count how many beads are on each side: one, two, three, four, five, six, seven. There are seven beads on each side. So, squaring the sum of four plus three is exactly equal to seven squared!"
Fig. 1: Binomial Square of Beads \((4 + 3)^2\) Visual representation of \((4+3)^2\) showing \(4^2\), two groups of \(4 \times 3\), and \(3^2\) built of yellow and pink glass beads.
  1. Write the algebraic expression on paper step-by-step. Encourage children to participate as you solve the value of each term. "Now, let's write down what we've done. We started with \((4 + 3)^2\), which means \((4 + 3) \times (4 + 3)\). When we multiply it out, we get four times four, plus four times three, plus three times four, plus three times three. Since four times three and three times four are equivalent, we can write two times four times three:"
    $$(4 + 3)^2 = 4^2 + 2(4 \times 3) + 3^2$$ $$16 + 24 + 9 = 49$$
  2. Invite the children to build their own squares using different sums, write down the equations, and verify the total area.

PRESENTATION B: Squaring a Trinomial

  1. Gather the children around the mat. Lay out the cards or visual aids representing the numbers 2 (green), 3 (pink), and 5 (light blue). Set up the trinomial problem as \((2 + 3 + 5)^2\). "Today we are going to focus on squaring a trinomial, using the numbers two, three, and five. To begin, let's focus on the first multiplier term: two. We'll turn over the cards showing three and five for a moment."
  2. Turn over cards 3 and 5. Place the green 2-square in the top-left corner. Next, multiply the remaining terms by 2: lay out 3 taken 2 times, then 5 taken 2 times. "What do we do first? It's two times two, which gives us two squared (green). Next, we multiply three by two, meaning three taken two times. Then, we have five by two—five taken two times. Now we are finished with our first multiplier row."
  3. Reveal card 3. Guide the children to multiply each term by 3: lay out 2 taken 3 times, 3 taken 3 times, and 5 taken 3 times. "Let's show our second multiplier, which is three. First, we have two taken three times. Next is three times three—so we place our pink three squared card here. Finally, we have five taken three times."
  4. Reveal card 5. Complete the final multiplication row: lay out 2 taken 5 times, 3 taken 5 times, and 5 taken 5 times. "Now we look at our third multiplier, which is five. We multiply two taken five times, then three taken five times, and finally five times five—which we represent with our light blue five squared card."
  5. Perform bead bar exchanges to achieve the most efficient representation. Exchange equivalent rectangular areas to use the fewest number of bars (e.g., swapping three 2-bars for two 3-bars). "Let's look at our layout. Next to two squared, we have three taken two times. Down here, we have two taken three times. Since they are equivalent, let's exchange them so we have pink three-bars on both sides. This uses the fewest physical bars while keeping our pattern beautiful. Let's do the same for our other rectangles: we will exchange our twos and threes for light blue five-bars."
  6. Verify that the three perfect squares—\(2^2\), \(3^2\), and \(5^2\)—run perfectly along the diagonal. Count the total outer dimensions to confirm a \(10 \times 10\) square.

PRESENTATION C: Algebraic Expression of the Square of a Binomial and Trinomial

Exercise 1 — Algebraic Expression of a Binomial

  1. Invite the children to lay out the binomial beads representing \(4 + 3\) again. Place a blank ticket labeled 'a' over the 3-beads, and a ticket labeled 'b' over the 4-beads. "Let's represent our squaring process algebraically. We will substitute our numbers with letters. Let's call all the threes 'a' and all the fours 'b'. Let's place a ticket 'a' above each three-bar and a ticket 'b' above each four-bar."
  2. Point to each section of beads and call out its algebraic equivalent term. "The first corner is \(a \times a\), which is \(a^2\). Next, we have \(a \times b\), and another \(a \times b\). Finally, we have \(b \times b\), which is \(b^2\)."
  3. Write out the expanded algebraic terms horizontally on paper. Combine the two middle identical terms. "Let's write down what we have constructed on our paper: our square is \(a^2 + ab + ab + b^2\). Since we have two \(ab\) terms, we combine them to write the final formula:"
    $$(a + b)^2 = a^2 + 2ab + b^2$$
  4. Introduce the vertical multiplication of a binomial by a binomial. Explain how this aligns with the long multiplication process they already know. "In mathematics, we can also multiply these polynomials vertically. Let's write them down just like we do with our multi-digit multiplication problems:"
    \(\begin{array}{r} a + b \\ \times \quad a + b \\ \hline ab + b^2 \\ a^2 + ab \phantom{+ b^2} \\ \hline a^2 + 2ab + b^2 \end{array}\)
    "Look how our vertical multiplication steps match the rows of beads on our mat exactly!"

Exercise 2 — Algebraic Expression of a Trinomial

  1. Set up the trinomial expression \((2 + 3 + 4)^2\) using physical beads and visual cards on the mat. "Now we are ready to find the algebraic expression of a trinomial squared. Let's build our square using the values two, three, and four."
  2. Perform the multiplication step-by-step for each row, placing corresponding letter tickets (assigning \(a = 2\), \(b = 3\), and \(c = 4\)). "Let's substitute our numbers with letters: all the twos are 'a', all the threes are 'b', and all the fours are 'c'. Let's label our pieces using these tickets."
  3. Lay out the tickets row-by-row on the mat and write the full expanded polynomial expression on paper. "Let's write down each row systematically: our first row is \(a^2 + ab + ac\). Our second row is \(ab + b^2 + bc\). Our third row is \(ac + bc + c^2\). When we write them down and group our identical terms, we get:"
    $$(a + b + c)^2 = a^2 + 2ab + 2ac + b^2 + 2bc + c^2$$
  4. Introduce the vertical multiplication of a trinomial by a trinomial to represent the final formula. "Let's write this multiplication out vertically to see how we collect our terms:"
    \(\begin{array}{r} a + b + c \\ \times \quad a + b + c \\ \hline ac + bc + c^2 \\ ab + b^2 + bc \phantom{+ c^2} \\ a^2 + ab + ac \phantom{+ bc + c^2} \\ \hline a^2 + 2ab + 2ac + b^2 + 2bc + c^2 \end{array}\)
  5. Encourage children to substitute numbers back into the letters to prove the equation remains constant. Suggest using graph paper to organize their equations.

Instructor's Aside & Album Notes

  • Creative Freedom: This exercise allows children to transcend the standard squares of the bead cabinet by constructing unique squares of any customized size (using any combinations from 1 to 9).
  • Pacing and Transitions: If the children are already highly familiar with squaring transformations, they can quickly progress to writing equations on paper. It is not necessary to build every single problem using physical beads once the abstract symbolic connection is secure.
  • Simplifying with Commutativity: During bead exchanges, swapping out matching pairs of rectangles (such as replacing three 2-bars with two 3-bars) demonstrates both commutativity (\(ab = ba\)) and structural resource optimization.
  • The Vertical Multiplication Method: This lesson is the ideal moment to introduce the vertical polynomial multiplication format, providing a solid mathematical bridge for the children's upcoming work with square roots and larger algebraic systems.
