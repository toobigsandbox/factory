Mathematics — Ratio and Proportion

1. Introduction to Ratio

Guide: Elim Lee Lesson 1 of 5

Direct Aim / Purpose: To introduce the concept and notation of ratio.

Materials List

  • Work mat
  • Objects from the environment: some beads from racks & tubes, some sticks from the geometry box of sticks, pencils & colored pencils, or small items from around the classroom
  • Felt square from the Introduction to Decimal Checkerboard (optional, to serve as a high-contrast layout space)
  • Blank paper tickets and pencils for labeling

Presentation

Invite a group of children to a work mat, stating that today we will examine relationships between groups of objects in our environment.

Gather two distinct sets of items. For this demonstration, we will use three standard pencils and two red colored pencils. Place them on the felt mat.

Arrange the objects clearly: place the three standard pencils in a horizontal line, and directly opposite or below them, place the two red colored pencils.

"Let's look closely at these groups of pencils. We have three standard pencils here, and we have two colored pencils here. For every three standard pencils we have, there are always two colored pencils."

"This shows a unique relationship between our standard pencils and colored pencils. We say that the relationship between them is three to two."

Write the number "3" on a small ticket and place it next to the standard pencils. Write the number "2" on a second ticket and place it next to the colored pencils.

"We call this mathematical relationship a 'ratio.' A ratio is a direct way of comparing two numbers, quantities, or objects."

Now write the ratio notation onto a single ticket. Write "3 : 2" with the colon centered between them. Place it underneath both groups.

"To write this relationship down mathematically, we use two stacked dots between our numbers. We read this written sign, $3 : 2$, as 'three to two.' The first number represents our three standard pencils, and the second number represents our two colored pencils."

Figure 1.1: Environment layout comparing 3 standard pencils to 2 colored pencils.

Emphasize the extreme importance of order when writing ratios.

"What would happen if we wrote this ratio as $2 : 3$? That would imply we have two standard pencils for every three colored pencils. But that is not what we have on our mat. So, order is extremely important when we write and discuss ratios. We must always match the numbers to the order of the objects we are comparing."

Demonstrate another example using different colored beads or blocks (e.g., 5 blue beads and 4 red beads). Have the children determine the ratio, write out the numbers on tickets, place the dots, and read the ratio aloud.

Invite the children to search around the environment to find and document their own ratios (e.g., chairs to tables, books to boxes).

Teacher's Aside & Practical Notes

This is the child's very first contact with ratio. It must remain highly sensory and integrated into their immediate surroundings. Emphasize that a ratio is fundamentally a multiplicative comparison of two groups rather than a simple difference. Allow the child to discover ratios across the classroom to internalize that the concept can be applied to any quantifiable elements.

2. Equivalent Ratios

Guide: Elim Lee Lesson 2 of 5

Direct Aim / Purpose: To show the relationship of equivalent ratios and introduce technical vocabulary (antecedent & consequent).

Materials List

  • Standard Montessori Pegboard
  • Small colored pegs (red, blue, green, etc.)
  • Number cards or blank tickets
  • Paper and pencils

Presentation

Set up the pegboard between the guide and the children. Place a collection of red and blue pegs in nearby bowls.

Place two red pegs vertically in the first column, and three blue pegs vertically in the adjacent column.

"Let us say we have a ratio of two red pencils to three blue pencils. Can you picture that in your head? If we imagine that these pegs represent our pencils, we can lay out two red pegs and three blue pegs. Here is our ratio of two to three."

"What this ratio tells us is that for every two red pegs we have, we must have exactly three blue pegs to keep our relationship. This is our pattern rule."

Now place another set of two red pegs and three blue pegs directly alongside the first set on the pegboard.

"Let us look at what we have now. How many red pegs do we have in total? We have four. And how many blue pegs do we have? We have six."

Place a third group of two red pegs and three blue pegs on the board.

"And now? How many red pegs total do I have? I have six. And how many blue pegs do we have? We have nine. Are they still in a two to three ratio? How do you know?"

"Yes, because for every two red ones, there are exactly three blue ones. Could we keep going? What would we do next? We would simply place three more blue pegs for every two red pegs."

Figure 2.1: Pegboard layout showcasing sequential scaling of $2:3$ to $4:6$.

Clear the board and introduce a new pattern with a different ratio.

"Let us try another ratio. What if instead we had a ratio of four to six?"

Assign color responsibilities to the children. For instance, ask one child to be in charge of the "fours" using green pegs, and another child to be in charge of the "sixes" using yellow pegs.

Have the children place four green pegs in one column and six yellow pegs in the second column.

"If you want to place another group of four green pegs, what must happen in the adjacent column? How many green pegs and yellow pegs do we have in total now?"

Let the children build out the $4:6$ ratio progressively on the board, counting up their totals as they go ($8:12$, $12:18$, etc.).

Repeat this with a few other ratios (e.g., $7$ to $2$) to build operational fluency. Once the children are comfortable, introduce the technical terminology.

"When we express a ratio mathematically, both numbers have specific names. The very first number that is stated in our ratio is called the antecedent. In our ratio of four to six, four is our antecedent."

"The second number in our ratio is called the consequent. In our ratio of four to six, six is our consequent."

"As you work with your peer partners on ratios today, check in with each other by asking: 'What is the consequent in your ratio?' or 'What is the antecedent in your ratio?' Let us practice using these words together!"

Teacher's Aside & Practical Notes

The principal direct aim here is to show that a ratio can be scaled up or down (by multiplying or dividing both terms by the same factor) while maintaining an equivalent relationship.

Pedagogical tip: Do not always position the smaller number on the left (antecedent). Move it from side to side during exercises to encourage flexible mathematical thinking. Ensure children use the terms antecedent and consequent explicitly during their work!

3. Word Problems with Ratio

Guide: Elim Lee Lesson 3 of 5

Direct Aim / Purpose: To practice the application of ratios through hands-on word problems, demonstrating how to solve for unknown quantities visually and algebraically.

Materials List

  • Pegboard & pegs (at least two colors)
  • Number cards and blank tickets
  • Notebook paper, grid paper, and pencils

Presentation

Introduce a written word problem to the children:

"The ratio of two numbers is $2$ to $4$. The smaller of the two numbers is $8$. What is the larger number?"

"What do we already know from this problem? Yes, we know the initial ratio is two to four."

Place two green pegs in one column (antecedent) and four orange pegs in the next column (consequent).

"What is the smaller of our two numbers right now? It is two. But our problem tells us that the smaller number needs to be eight. So, for every group of two pegs we place in our antecedent column, we must place a group of four pegs in our consequent column. Do we have eight pegs in our antecedent column yet? No, we only have two. Let's keep building."

Place another group of two green pegs and four orange pegs. Repeat this step until the green column contains exactly eight pegs.

Figure 3.1: Visual representation of a scaled ratio column, pairing groups of 2 to groups of 4.

"Look at our antecedent column. How many green pegs do we have now? We have exactly eight. Our problem stated that the smaller of our two numbers is eight. Have we carefully followed our ratio of two to four while building this board? Yes, we have."

"Now, all we have to do to find our answer is count how many orange pegs we have in our consequent column. Let us count them together."

Count the orange pegs aloud with the children: "1, 2, 3... 16."

"Sixteen! That is absolutely correct. The larger number in our ratio is 16."

Transition to a second problem, reversing the known term:

"A ratio is given as $2$ to $3$. This time you know the larger number is $9$. What is the smaller number?"

"What is our ratio? Two to three. And which is the larger number in our written ratio? Three. But we know our actual larger number needs to be nine. Let us construct this with our pegs."

Have the children build out the columns of 2 and 3 sequentially until the consequent column reaches exactly 9 pegs. Ask them to count the resulting antecedent pegs.

"We reached nine pegs in our consequent. How many pegs do we have on the other side? Six! Absolutely correct. Let us write this problem down on our paper to analyze the math we just did."

Write the calculations clearly on paper as shown below:

Ratio: 2 : 4 ==> Smaller number is 8.
2 x 4 = 8 (We multiplied our antecedent by 4)
4 x 4 = 16 (So we must multiply our consequent by 4)
Resulting Ratio: 8 : 16

"We had a ratio of $2 : 4$. To turn our antecedent of $2$ into our target number of $8$, what did we multiply by? We multiplied by $4$. What happens if we multiply our consequent of $4$ by that same number, $4$? We get $16$. This means we can find equivalent ratios simply by multiplying both our antecedent and consequent by the same number!"

Provide an abstract word problem to reinforce this scaling principle without relying entirely on the pegboard:

"Suppose we had numbers in the ratio of $3$ to $7$, and the larger number is $21$. What is the smaller number?"

Write down "$3 : 7$" on paper.

"Which number in our ratio is the smaller one? Three is. The larger number in our ratio is seven, but our actual larger number is $21$. Is there a number we can multiply seven by to equal $21$?"

"Yes, we can multiply seven by three. If we multiply our consequent by three, we must also multiply our antecedent by three to preserve our ratio relationship. What is three times three? It is nine. Nine is our answer!"

Teacher's Aside & Practical Notes

It is completely fine if children prefer to lay out all 8 or 9 pegs for the known number tightly on the board first, and then build the opposite side group-by-group afterward. As long as they can clearly verbalize and explain their logical process, allow them to approach the physical material in a way that works for them.

Offering multiple paths to solve a problem accommodates diverse learners and respects their unique cognitive styles.

4. Applying Knowledge of Common Fractions

Guide: Elim Lee Lesson 4 of 5

Direct Aim / Purpose: To bridge the concept of ratios with common fractions, showing that ratios can be expressed and manipulated as fractional values.

Materials List

  • Pegboard and colored pegs
  • Number cards or tickets
  • Blank paper and writing instruments

Presentation

Place two green pegs and three blue pegs on the pegboard.

"Let us say we have a ratio of two green pegs to three blue pegs. Do you agree with this arrangement? What if I take these same pegs and move them into this clustered shape, do we still have a ratio of two to three? Yes, we do. The physical arrangement does not alter the relationship."

Arrange the pegs back in a row. Point to the green pegs and then the blue pegs.

"Let us look closely at our first row. Two is exactly two-thirds of three. If we imagine that each of these blue pegs represents one-third, we have one-third, two-thirds, three-thirds. This means our two green pegs represent two-thirds of our blue peg group."

Write down the ratio as a fraction: $\frac{2}{3}$.

"We write our ratio as 2 to 3. Think about our standard division sign. The division sign is actually written with a line and two dots: a dot on top and a dot on the bottom. Those dots represent our numbers! Ratio fundamentally means division. That is why the order of numbers is so important. $\frac{2}{3}$ is completely different from $\frac{3}{2}$."

Figure 4.1: Visualization of 2 green pegs representing $\frac{2}{3}$ of the 3 blue pegs.

"Now that we know we can write our ratios as common fractions, we can apply everything we already know about equivalent fractions to solve ratio problems!"

Write down the equation: $\frac{2}{3} = \frac{?}{9}$.

"Let us say we have a ratio of two to three, and we know our actual larger number is nine. Let us write that as a fraction. What must we do to this denominator of three to scale it up to nine? We multiply it by three."

"If we multiply our denominator by three, we must also multiply our numerator by three so that we do not change the core value of the fraction. What is two times three? It is six. Our new fraction is six-ninths, which we can write as the ratio $6 : 9$."

Verify this with the pegs on the pegboard, showing that scaling $2 : 3$ up by a factor of 3 results in 6 green pegs and 9 blue pegs.

Present another problem to practice:

"We have a ratio of $3$ to $2$, and our antecedent is $27$. What is our consequent?"

Write the fraction $\frac{3}{2}$ on paper.

"Let us write our ratio as a fraction: $\frac{3}{2}$. We know our antecedent (numerator) is $27$. Let's write $\frac{3}{2} = \frac{27}{?}$. What do we multiply three by to get $27$? We multiply by nine."

"Since we multiplied our numerator by nine, we must multiply our denominator by nine as well. What is two times nine? It is $18$. Our answer is $\frac{27}{18}$, or a ratio of $27$ to $18$."

Teacher's Aside & Practical Notes

Connecting ratios to common fractions is a major milestone of mathematical abstraction. After this lesson, children will often choose to perform ratio calculations abstractly using fractions first, and then use the physical pegboard solely to self-correct and verify their work. Encourage this step toward abstraction!

5. More Word Problems with Ratio

Guide: Elim Lee Lesson 5 of 5

Direct Aim / Purpose: To solve complex, multi-step ratio word problems (including sums and differences) using both visual materials and fractional equations.

Materials List

  • Pegboard and colored pegs
  • Number cards or tickets
  • Blank paper and pencils

Presentation

Introduce the first complex problem:

"A man left some money when he died to be distributed to his only child and his wife in a ratio of $2$ to $3$. The child received $\$2,400$. How much did the wife receive?"

"What is our ratio? Two to three. Let us write that down. We also know the child receives $\$2,400$. To represent this on our pegboard, let's agree that each peg represents $\$100$."

Place two pegs in the antecedent column to represent the child's initial $\$200$.

"Here, the child has received two hundred dollars. To reach twenty-four hundred dollars, we need $24$ pegs in this column. Let us lay those out."

Place $24$ pegs in the antecedent column, placing $3$ corresponding pegs in the consequent column for every $2$ antecedent pegs placed.

"We know that for every $\$200$ the child gets, the wife gets $\$300$. Now that we have placed all of our pegs, let us count the wife's total pegs."

Count the wife's pegs. There are $36$ pegs in total.

"Thirty-six pegs! Since each peg represents $\$100$, the wife received $\$3,600$. Let us write this on our paper to verify."

Ratio = 2 : 3 ==> Child gets 2,400.
2 x 1200 = 2,400 (Child)
3 x 1200 = 3,600 (Wife)

Introduce the second problem involving a sum:

"Two numbers are in a ratio of $4$ to $7$. The sum of the two numbers is $55$. What are the two numbers?"

"Let us lay out our initial ratio of four to seven using pegs."

Place 4 green pegs in the first column and 7 yellow pegs in the second column.

"What is the sum of our two ratio numbers right now? Sum means we add them together. Four plus seven is $11$."

Write "11" on a small paper ticket and place it below the pegboard.

"But our problem says our total sum must be $55$. Let us add more groups of four and seven pegs until our total sum reaches $55$."

Have the children continue adding pegs (4 green, 7 yellow) and calculating the cumulative sum ($11 \rightarrow 22 \rightarrow 33 \rightarrow 44 \rightarrow 55$) until exactly 55 total pegs are on the board.

"Now we have reached $55$ pegs in total. Let us count the pegs in our columns. How many green pegs do we have? We have $20$. And how many yellow pegs do we have? We have $35$. Let us check our work on paper."

Ratio = 4 : 7 ==> Target Sum = 55.
Sum of ratio terms: 4 + 7 = 11.
How many groups of 11 are in 55? 55 / 11 = 5 groups.
Multiply both terms by 5:
4 x 5 = 20
7 x 5 = 35
Check sum: 20 + 35 = 55. Correct!
Figure 5.1: Proportional block representation showing how scaling by a factor of 5 achieves the target sum.

Introduce the third problem involving a difference:

"Two numbers are in a ratio of $3$ to $5$. The difference between the two numbers is $10$. What are the two numbers?"

"Let us lay out our initial ratio of three to five on the board."

Place 3 blue pegs and 5 red pegs.

"What is the difference between these two columns right now? Difference means subtraction. Five minus three is two. But our problem states that the difference must be $10$. Let us keep building our ratio and calculating the difference at each step."

Place a second group of pegs (making 6 and 10). Calculate the difference: $10 - 6 = 4$. Place a third group (making 9 and 15). Calculate: $15 - 9 = 6$. Place a fourth group (making 12 and 20). Calculate: $20 - 12 = 8$. Place a fifth group (making 15 and 25). Calculate: $25 - 15 = 10$.

"We reached our target difference of $10$! What are our two numbers? We have $15$ blue pegs and $25$ red pegs. Let us write this out on paper to see if our math aligns."

Ratio = 3 : 5 ==> Target Difference = 10.
Difference of ratio terms: 5 - 3 = 2.
How many groups of 2 are in our target difference of 10? 10 / 2 = 5 groups.
Multiply both terms by 5:
3 x 5 = 15
5 x 5 = 25
Check difference: 25 - 15 = 10. Correct!

Teacher's Aside & Practical Notes

If a child immediately calculates the correct multiplier abstractly (e.g., realizing they need 5 groups), invite them to explain their reasoning to their peers.

Pedagogical tip: Educated guesses and mental math engage the child's reasoning mind beautifully and are completely valid problem-solving strategies. Even if the child figures out the multiplier quickly, guiding them through the concrete step-by-step subtraction visually helps anchor the concept and reinforces their fundamental skills.
