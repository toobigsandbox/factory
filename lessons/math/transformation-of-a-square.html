Subject Area: Mathematics Guide Name: Elim Lee

Transformation of a Square

Chapter III: Squares and Cubes of Numbers

Direct Aim

For the children to recognize that the geometric pattern of a square transformed into a binomial (sum of 2 terms) is 2 squares along the diagonal and 2 rectangles filling out the rest of the square. [Trinomial is the sum of 3 terms with 3 squares along the diagonal and 6 rectangles.]

Indirect Aim

To introduce the language: binomial, trinomial, quadrinomial, polynomial. To prepare for understanding square roots and squaring numbers of larger sizes.

Materials

  • Felt mat (neutral background)
  • Colored rubber bands / small hair ties
  • Bead Squares and Bead Bars of the Cabinet (1–10 Scale)
  • Binomial Plate & Trinomial Plate (Wooden geometric representations)
  • Paper, pencil, & colored pencils matching the Montessori Bead colors (1: Red, 2: Green, 3: Pink, 4: Yellow, 5: Light Blue, 6: Purple, 7: White, 8: Brown, 9: Dark Blue, 10: Gold)
  • 1 in. square paper
  • Printed square of beads (100 circles)
  • Graph paper & straight edge
  • Tickets (blank and prepared with terms), scissors, operation signs (\(+\)), and prepared parentheses slips

PRESENTATION A: Transformation of a Square Into a Binomial

Exercise 1 — Square of Ten Transformed into a Binomial

  1. Show all the materials clearly to the children before beginning. Lay them out on the felt mat so each child can see and access them. Prepare the paper tickets and scissors. "Today, we are starting a new chapter on the squaring of numbers and the transformation of squares. This work builds directly on what we learned previously about squares and cubes and is a preparation for understanding square roots. We will begin by revisiting what makes a square."
  2. First, assemble a small group of children and present the materials. Hold up the golden 10-bead square. "I wonder how many smaller squares are inside this 10 square?"
  3. Take the 10-bead square and place it on the felt mat. Use a rubber band to mark off a \(4 \times 4\) section (forming the four-square). Explain that 4 is represented by yellow beads in our cabinet. Slowly count aloud as you stretch the rubber band: "One, two, three, four. See how this yellow four-square fits inside the larger square?"
  4. Now, let's look at the remaining space along the diagonal. Take another rubber band and stretch it across to mark off the rest. From the opposite corner of the diagonal, count off a \(6 \times 6\) square (which is represented by the purple beads). "One, two, three, four, five, six. This is our purple six-square. So, inside my 10-square, I have a yellow four-square and a purple six-square. Let's see what is filling the rest of the space."
  5. Point out the two remaining rectangular spaces. Count their dimensions to show they are composed of matching colored bars. "We have two rectangles here. Let's see what they are: this one is six by four, and this one is four by six. Notice how they are composed of our yellow four-bars and purple six-bars laid together. These rectangles are identical, just flipped. From this 10-square, we've made a yellow four-square, a purple six-square, and two identical rectangles."
  6. Use the rubber bands to clearly highlight the boundaries on the bead square. Invite the children to observe the layout closely.
Fig. 1: Sensorial Binomial Bead Square Transformation \((4 + 6)^2\) Visual representation of the \(10\)-bead square divided sensorially using Yellow (4) and Purple (6) cabinet bead bars.
  1. Invite children to discover other squares inside the 10-square. "Now, I wonder what other squares we can find inside this 10-square? I invite you to explore this on your own. You don't always have to start with the smallest squares. You can start with a larger square like seven (white) or eight (brown)."
  2. Encourage free exploration. Support them by asking open-ended questions like "What shapes do you see?" and "Can you find another perfect square inside this one?"

Exercise 2 — Verbalizing and Writing the Binomial

  1. Show materials on screen or workspace: paper, pencil, blank tickets, scissors, and slips of paper. Explain that we will now write down what we have done. "What is the value of this whole 10-square? Yes, it is 100 because each side is length 10. I am going to write on my paper: \(10^2 = \)"
  2. Model writing the formula step-by-step. Turn the paper landscape to allow a long layout. "We made smaller squares: \(4^2\) and \(6^2\), and rectangles: \(4 \times 6\) and \(6 \times 4\). So we write: \(10^2 = (4 + 6)^2\). What does that equal? The first square is \(4^2\), then \(6^2\), plus the rectangles \(4 \times 6\) and \(6 \times 4\) which are identical."
  3. Write out the full expanded mathematical expression:
    $$10^2 = (4+6)^2 = 4^2 + (4 \times 6) + (6 \times 4) + 6^2$$
  4. Bring the blank tickets. Write the terms on separate tickets and place them on top of the corresponding sections on the bead square. "Let's write a ticket for each part: we have a ticket for '4²', a ticket for '6²', and tickets for '4 × 6' and '6 × 4'."
  5. Explain that because \(4 \times 6\) and \(6 \times 4\) are equal, we can simplify this expression. Put a ticket showing "2(4 × 6)" over them. "Since they are the same, we have two of them! We write: \(4^2 + 2(4 \times 6) + 6^2\)."
  6. Work out the calculations with the children:
    $$4^2 = 16$$ $$2 \times (4 \times 6) = 2 \times 24 = 48$$ $$6^2 = 36$$ $$16 + 48 + 36 = 100$$
    "Adding these values together: 16 plus 48 plus 36. This equals exactly 100!"
  7. Model writing these addition processes vertically for clarity and comfort. Step back and encourage children to write other binomial combinations they discover.

PRESENTATION B: Transformation of a Square Into a Trinomial

  1. Prepare a 10 by 10 square of beads. Introduce the concept of turning this square into three smaller diagonal squares—a trinomial (sum of 3 terms) using the 2, 3, and 5 bead bars. "Today, we are going to try turning our 10 by 10 square into three squares. Remember, our 2-bead bar is green, our 3-bead bar is pink, and our 5-bead bar is light blue. These three diagonal squares must lie along the diagonal."
  2. Count out the first square of 2 from the top-left: one, two. Mark the \(2 \times 2\) green square with a rubber band. Place a ticket '2²' on it. "Here is our first square: two squared, which is green."
  3. Count out the next square along the diagonal, which will be of size 3: one, two, three. Mark this \(3 \times 3\) pink square. Place a ticket '3²' on it. "The second square is three squared, colored pink."
  4. The final square in the bottom-right corner is of size 5: one, two, three, four, five. Mark this \(5 \times 5\) light blue square and label it '5²'. "We have three squares along the diagonal: 2² (green), 3² (pink), and 5² (light blue)."
  5. Invite the children to observe the rectangles filling in the spaces between these diagonal squares. Place labels on each rectangle. "Let's see the rectangles that are generated: we have a 2 by 3 rectangle and a 3 by 2 rectangle. We also have a 2 by 5 rectangle and a 5 by 2. Finally, we have a 3 by 5 and a 5 by 3 rectangle."
Fig. 2: Sensorial Trinomial Bead Square Transformation \((2 + 3 + 5)^2\) Visual layout of the trinomial bead square \((2 + 3 + 5)^2\) incorporating Green (2), Pink (3), and Light Blue (5) categories.
  1. Write out the full expanded expression for the trinomial together on paper:
    $$10^2 = (2 + 3 + 5)^2 = 2^2 + 2(2 \times 3) + 2(2 \times 5) + 3^2 + 2(3 \times 5) + 5^2$$
  2. Combine like terms to simplify and find the value of each term:
    $$2^2 = 4$$ $$2 \times (2 \times 3) = 12$$ $$2 \times (2 \times 5) = 20$$ $$3^2 = 9$$ $$2 \times (3 \times 5) = 30$$ $$5^2 = 25$$ $$\text{Sum} = 4 + 12 + 20 + 9 + 30 + 25 = 100$$
  3. Encourage children to explore with different trinomial values on their own, finding identical pairs of rectangles to combine.

PRESENTATION C: Printed Squares for the Binomial and Trinomial

  1. Show the printed squares containing 100 circles each (representing the bead grid) and three colored pencils in contrasting colors. "Today we will explore the squaring transformation using printed squares. This serves as a wonderful way to record our findings. Remember, all squares must sit along the diagonal."
  2. Count out a binomial size (e.g., 4 and 6). Choose the first color matching the Bead Cabinet (Yellow for 4) and color the circles one by one to form the \(4 \times 4\) square. "We color them circle by circle instead of crossing out areas, to build accuracy. Let's make a beautiful yellow 4 by 4 square here in the corner."
  3. Identify the starting circle for the next square along the diagonal. Choose the second color matching the Cabinet (Purple for 6) and color the \(6 \times 6\) square.
  4. Use the third color (e.g., Gold/Amber) to color the remaining rectangles. Emphasize that because the rectangles are matching pairs, they are colored identical. "Our remaining shapes are two rectangles: a 4 by 6 and a 6 by 4. They are equivalent in size, so we color them with the same third color."
  5. For the trinomial, introduce a second printed sheet. Encourage the children to select three contrasting colors for the squares along the diagonal, and coordinate colors for matching rectangle pairs.
  6. Encourage children to outline each shape first before coloring inside to ensure counting accuracy and prevent smudging.

PRESENTATION D: Transformation of Squares Done on Squared Paper

  1. Display full sheets of graph paper, a straight edge (ruler), a pencil, and colored pencils. "We can also construct our squaring transformations on squared paper. This frees us from the limits of our physical material—we can make squares of any size, even with four or more terms!"
  2. Propose building a binomial square representing \((4 + 8)^2\). Point out that this total size (12 units) would not fit on the 10-bead square material.
  3. Start near the top-left of the graph paper. Make a small dot. Count 4 units (Yellow scale) to the right and place a dot, then count 8 more units (Brown scale) to the right and place a dot.
  4. Do the same vertically: count 4 units down, place a dot; then count 8 units down and place a dot. Use the ruler to draw straight lines connecting these coordinates to complete the grid.
Fig. 3: Graph Paper Construction of Binomial \((4 + 8)^2\) Graph paper layout showing binomial partitioning of \((4+8)^2\). Blue lines depict grid divisions.
  1. Color each smaller square and rectangle lightly using contrasting colored pencils. Write the values within each part. "Here we have a four-square, an eight-square, and two rectangles of size four by eight. Let's write down the value of each: \(4^2 = 16\), \(8^2 = 64\), and each rectangle has an area of \(32\)."
  2. Explain that this hands-on paper construction helps solidify the structural pattern of squared numbers:
    $$(4 + 8)^2 = 4^2 + 2(4 \times 8) + 8^2$$ $$12^2 = 16 + 64 + 64 = 144$$
  3. Encourage children to cut out these shapes, glue them onto cardboard or paper, label the mathematical names, and explore larger sizes.

PRESENTATION E: Algebraic Notation

Exercise 1 — Binomial Algebraic Notation

  1. Clear the felt mat. Place the 10-bead square in the center. Gather the children around, and place blank paper tickets and operation signs. "Today, we are going to connect our numbers to letters. This is the beginning of algebra! Algebra is simply about using a letter to represent any number."
  2. Form a binomial square division (e.g., 7 and 3) using a rubber band. "Instead of calling this first dimension seven, we are going to call it 'a'. So this square of seven is now 'a squared'. Let's write 'a²' on our ticket and place it right here."
  3. Write 'a²' on a blank ticket and place it on the \(7 \times 7\) square.
  4. Indicate the smaller diagonal square (size 3). "Instead of calling this length three, we will call it 'b'. So this square is 'b squared'. Let's write 'b²' on this ticket and place it on the square."
  5. Now point to the two rectangles. Explain how their dimensions are determined by the side lengths of the squares. "This rectangle is 'a' by 'b'. In algebra, when we write letters multiplied together, we omit the multiplication sign. So we just write 'ab'. The other rectangle is also 'ab'."
  6. Write 'ab' on two separate tickets and place them on the rectangles.
Fig. 4: Algebraic Wooden Binomial Plate Colors Color-coded Binomial Plate layout: \(a^2\) (Red), \(b^2\) (Blue), and product rectangles (Black with coded boundaries).
  1. Gather the tickets and organize them in a line on the felt mat to build a formal algebraic expression. "Let's line our tickets up: first 'a²', then add 'ab', then add another 'ab', and finally 'b²'."
  2. Group the two matching 'ab' tickets. Write the simplified formula on paper.
    $$(a + b)^2 = a^2 + 2ab + b^2$$
    "This is our universal algebraic formula for the square of a binomial! It doesn't matter what numbers we substitute for 'a' and 'b'—this formula will always tell us the correct area."
  3. Guide the children to check the formula by substituting real values (such as \(a = 7\) and \(b = 3\)). Point out how the numbers fit and calculate exactly to 100.

Exercise 2 — Trinomial Algebraic Notation

  1. Assemble the group. Set up the 10-bead square partitioned into three sections (2, 3, and 5) with rubber bands. "Now we are ready for the trinomial! Let's rename our three diagonal dimensions as 'A', 'B', and 'C'."
  2. Invite a student to write out the tickets for the diagonal squares using algebraic letters: "Instead of 2, 3, and 5, our diagonal squares are 'A²', 'B²', and 'C²'."
  3. Place these tickets on the corresponding squares. Then ask the children to label the rectangular regions by looking at their sides: "This rectangle matches A and B, so it is 'AB'. We have another matching 'AB' here. This one is 'AC' and its pair is also 'AC'. Finally, we have two 'BC' rectangles. Place the tickets down on them."
  4. Remove the tickets from the board and lay them out on the mat, grouping matching terms side-by-side.
  5. Write out the full algebraic equation for a trinomial on the board or paper:
    $$(A + B + C)^2 = A^2 + 2AB + 2AC + B^2 + 2BC + C^2$$
  6. Point out that the squares run precisely along the diagonal. Invite children to substitute their own numbers to prove that the formula is truly universal.

Instructor's Aside & Album Notes — Color Systems in Squaring

Within AMI elementary math development, squaring is explored across three consecutive physical material progressions, each utilizing a specific color scheme:

1. Bead Cabinet (Sensorial)

Uses bead bars colored by unit length to represent concrete dimensions: 1: Red, 2: Green, 3: Pink, 4: Yellow, 5: Light Blue, 6: Purple, 7: White, 8: Brown, 9: Dark Blue, 10: Gold.

2. Wood Plates (Algebraic)

Wooden pieces for the Binomial & Trinomial layout representing algebraic indices: \(a^2\) is solid Red, \(b^2\) is Blue, and \(c^2\) is Yellow. Non-squared rectangles are black with matching dimensional border colors.

3. Peg Board (Hierarchical)

For squaring multi-digit integers with place value categories: Units (and millions) = Green, Tens (and ten-thousands) = Blue, Hundreds (and hundred-thousands) = Red.

  • Pedagogical Context: This is a key introductory chapter in the elementary mathematics curriculum. It comes prior to powers of two, signed/negative numbers, decimal fractions, and non-decimal number bases. It runs parallel to the work with multiples and factors.
  • The Role of Handwork: Handwork is crucial to the internalization of these concepts. Cutting, gluing, coloring, and labeling help build deep mental models that translate concrete visual geometry into abstract algebraic expressions.
  • Generalizations on Squaring:
    • All square categories lie exclusively along the grid's diagonal.
    • Other areas are filled in by matching pairs of rectangles generated by the diagonal squares.
    • A binomial square consists of \(2^2 = 4\) geometric pieces (2 squares, 2 rectangles).
    • A trinomial square consists of \(3^2 = 9\) geometric pieces (3 squares, 6 rectangles).
    • A quadrinomial square consists of \(4^2 = 16\) geometric pieces. The total number of pieces is always the square of the number of terms: \(n^2\).
