Math // Square Roots

Extracting Square Root with the Square Root Board

Guide: Elim Lee // AMI Elementary

Purpose

To show the children how to extract square roots of numbers up to 225 using the square root board, and to introduce the concept of a remainder.

Material Analysis

  • • Square root board (15x15 divots)
  • • Container with at least 125 green unit beads (ideally 225)
  • • Small dish or pottery bowl
  • • Felt mat
  • • Paper and pencil

Presentation

Gather a group. Show the green unit beads in the container and the square root board.
- "Today, we're going to learn how to extract square roots using this square root board."
State the problem: Find the square root of 14.
- "What is the square root of 14? We don't have a bead square for 14, but we can find it. First, let's count 14 beads into our bowl."
Count 14 beads precisely. Set the container aside.
Place one bead in the top-left corner (the square of 1).
- "Can we build a bigger square? Let's extend by one bead in each direction and complete it."
Build the 2x2 square (adding 3 beads). Then attempt the 3x3 square (adding 5 beads).
- "Now we have a square of three. Can I make a square of four? Let's check our bowl."
Demonstrate that only 5 beads remain—not enough to complete a 4x4 square (which needs 7 more beads).
- "I can't make a square of four. So, the biggest complete square we can make with 14 is 3 by 3. The square root of 14 is 3, with 5 left over. That leftover is called the remainder."
Record: $\sqrt{14} = 3 \text{ r } 5$.
Repeat with 35. Count 35 beads. Build until the 5x5 square is complete.
- "Can I make the square of six? No, I don't have enough. So the square root of 35 is 5, with a remainder of 10."
"Don’t rush past this sensorial stage. Let children enjoy the repetition with numbers like 66 or 77—numbers that feel 'large' to them but remain manageable. Try counting out 210 beads and making a square on graph paper yourself to appreciate the effort this requires without the beads."
Elim Lee // April 1st Milestone Edition
