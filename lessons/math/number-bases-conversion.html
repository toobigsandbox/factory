Mathematics // Advanced Arithmetic

Number Bases Conversion

Guide: Elim Lee // AMI Elementary Submission

Aims

To explore numerical systems other than base-10 and understand the logic of place value through conversion.

Materials

Multi-base material (wooden base blocks), graph paper, colored pencils.

Presentation I: Binary Exploration (Base 2)

Invite the child and set out the units and bars for Base 2.
"In our decimal system, we stop at nine and then we have to move to the next wire. In Base 2, we can only use two digits: zero and one."
Fig 1.1: Base 2 (Binary) Material Progression
Show the value of 5 in Base 10. Ask how to represent it in Base 2.
"We have one four, zero twos, and one unit. So five in our system looks like 101 in binary."

Presentation II: Conversion from Base 10

Write a number (e.g., 13) and the target base (Base 3).
"To find out how many groups of three we have, we will divide by our target base."
Perform repeated division by 3, recording the remainders.
Fig 2.1: Repeated Division Algorithm (The Remainder Method)
"Thirteen divided by three is four, with a remainder of one. Four divided by three is one, with a remainder of one. One divided by three is zero, with a remainder of one."

Presentation III: Conversion to Base 10

Write a number in a non-decimal base, such as 122 in Base 3.
"To bring this back to our familiar decimal language, we have to look at the value of each position."
Value 3² (9) 3¹ (3) 3⁰ (1)
Digit 1 2 2
Fig 3.1: Place Value Table for Base 3 Conversion
Label the positions: units ($3^0$), threes ($3^1$), and nines ($3^2$).
"We have one nine ($1 \times 9$), two threes ($2 \times 3$), and two units ($2 \times 1$). Nine plus six plus two equals seventeen."
