Geometry // Area

Area of a Circle

Guide: Elim Lee // AMI Elementary

Purpose

To derive the formula for the area of a circle by transforming divided circle pieces into a "wavy rectangle" and linking geometric intuition with algebraic expression.

Material Analysis

  • • Large envelope with tickets, fraction bar, rectangles, and whole circles.
  • • Small envelope with divided circles (one blue, one yellow).
  • • Writing material (paper/pencil).

Presentation

Open the large envelope. Lay out the materials and introduce the topic.
- "Today, we're going to learn about the area of a circle. I have several pieces here to help us explore."
Invite two children to build the blue and yellow divided circles.
- "Who would like to build our blue divided circle? And the yellow? Now that we have built them, what do we know? Are they equivalent? Yes, they are!"
Reference the transformation of polygons (hexagon, triangle, trapezoid) into rectangles.
- "We transform shapes into rectangles because rectangles are easier to work with. I wonder if we can do the same with a circle."
Take the pieces apart and arrange them in an interlocking line, alternating the points.
- "Watch as I line these up. It doesn't look much like a rectangle yet, does it? But look—if we flip these smaller segments at the end, we get a nice straight line."
- "It's a wavy rectangle! If these segments got smaller and smaller, we would get closer to a perfect rectangle. What part of the circle gave us the base? The circumference! And the height? That's the radius!"
- "Because we used pieces from TWO circles to build this rectangle, we have to divide our answer by two."

Derivation Summary

Step Expression Explanation
Starting Point $\text{Area} \approx \text{Base} \times \text{Height}$ Approximating circle segments as a rectangle.
Dimensions $\text{Base} = C, \text{Height} = r$ Base is the total circumference of the circle.
Two-Circle Correction $\frac{C \times r}{2}$ Divided by 2 because the rectangle represents two circles.
Substitution $\frac{2\pi r \times r}{2}$ Since $C = 2\pi r$, we substitute it into the formula.
Final Formula $\pi r^2$ The 2s cancel out, leaving the standard formula.
"While the concept $A = \frac{C \times r}{2}$ is our sensorial starting point, the traditional formula $A = \pi r^2$ is preferred for its mathematical simplicity and universality. Both are correct, but the latter is what the child will use most often in future work."
