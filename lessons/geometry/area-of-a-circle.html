Geometry // Area

Area of a Circle

Guide: Elim Lee // AMI Elementary

Purpose

To derive the formula for the area of a circle by transforming divided circle pieces into a "wavy rectangle" and linking geometric intuition with algebraic expression.

Material Analysis

  • • Large envelope with tickets, fraction bar, and circles.
  • • Metal inset frames with divided circles (specifically 10-sector divisions).
  • • Blue and Yellow divided circles (each with 10 wedges).
  • • Writing material (paper/pencil).

Presentation

Lay out the materials. Show the two divided circles—one yellow and one blue—each in its frame.
- "Today, we're going to explore the area of a circle. We have these two circles to help us, each divided into ten equal pieces."
Invite the children to build the circles from the wedges. Confirm their equivalence.
- "Who would like to build the yellow circle? And the blue? What do we know about these? Are they the same size? Yes, they are equivalent."
Remove the wedges and arrange them in an interlocking row on the mat.
- "I'm going to take these pieces and put them in a line. Look at how they fit together... one pointing up, one pointing down."

Interlocking wedges from the blue and yellow 10-sector circles

- "It looks like a wavy rectangle, doesn't it? If we had many, many more pieces—if our segments were as thin as hairs—this line would be perfectly straight. We can use what we know about rectangles to find the area of our circle."
Point to the base and the height. Explain that the rectangle uses pieces from both circles.
- "The base of this rectangle is the circumference of one whole circle. The height is the radius. Since we used pieces from two circles, we must divide our total area by two to find the area of just one circle."

Derivation Summary

Step Expression Explanation
Approximation $\text{Area} \approx \text{Base} \times \text{Height}$ Transforming sectors into a rectangular form.
Identification $\text{Base} = C, \text{Height} = r$ The base equals the total circumference.
Two-Circle Adjustment $\frac{C \times r}{2}$ The rectangle represents two circles (Blue + Yellow).
Algebraic Substitution $\frac{2\pi r \times r}{2}$ Substitute $C = 2\pi r$.
Final Formula $\pi r^2$ Cancel the 2s to find the area of a single circle.
"The 10-sector metal inset is the perfect starting point for the reasoning mind. It is large enough to handle and small enough to see the 'wavy' reality before the abstraction of calculus (infinite segments) takes over. Always link the base back to the rolling circumference work they have previously completed."
Elim Lee // April 1st Milestone Edition
