Geometry // Further Explorations with Equivalence

Euclid's Plate: Reasoning Level

Guide: Elim Lee // AMI Elementary Submission

Material Analysis

Euclid's Plate (Reasoning Board): A forest-green wooden frame with a central right-angled triangle slot. It features sliding tracks at the top and bottom containing yellow and blue equivalent parallelograms, an empty slot for the blue square on the short leg, a yellow square inset on the long leg, and a red square built on the hypotenuse that is divided into two distinct red rectangles by extending the triangle's height line.

Purpose

To guide the child’s reasoning mind through Euclid’s theorem (the projection of the leg onto the hypotenuse) by proving that each leg's square is equivalent to the corresponding rectangle built from the hypotenuse and the projection of that leg.

Presentation I: Height Extension and Projection

Gather the children around Euclid's Plate. Trace the white central right-angled triangle.
- "We have looked at this plate before, and we have talked about it in reference to Pythagoras. We also talked a little bit about the story of Euclid and his important contributions to geometry."
Trace a line with your finger starting from the right-angle vertex down perpendicular to the hypotenuse, cutting straight through the red square below.
- "Euclid said that if you take the height of a right angle triangle and extend it down, you will cut through the square built on the hypotenuse."
- "This is our height here. If we imagine dropping a line all the way down from this vertex to the base of the square, we can see our height. If we take the height from this vertex and extend it to the base of the triangle, that is our height, and if we continue extending it down, we divide the square into two rectangles."
- "Euclid went on to say that this rectangle would be equivalent to the square on the shorter leg of the right angle triangle, and this rectangle would be equivalent to the square on the longer leg of the right angle triangle."
- "We are going to use our parallelograms to see if we can understand what he was describing. To do this, we need to remember something about parallelograms and rectangles. A rectangle and a parallelogram are equivalent if they have the same base and the same height. A square is simply a very special rectangle."

Presentation II: Equivalence of Yellow Square and Yellow Parallelogram

Slide the yellow parallelogram out of its top track. Place it side-by-side with the yellow square built on the triangle's long leg.
- "Let’s start with our yellow square and our yellow parallelogram. The first thing we want to check is whether the bases are the same length. They are. Here is my base, and here is my base. They are the same length. We can see that by aligning the pieces."
Orient the board sideways to trace the height. Slide the yellow square into the adjacent empty testing channel, followed by the yellow parallelogram.
- "Now let’s compare the heights. I will turn the frame and slide these over. We know the bases are equivalent, and now we are going to check whether the heights are equivalent."
- "I place the square here. The square fits exactly into this rectangle and has the same height. Now I place the parallelogram. It also fits exactly and has the same height. We know that this yellow square and this yellow parallelogram have the same base and the same height. They are equivalent."

Presentation III: Connecting to the Red Rectangle (Hypotenuse Projection)

Reset the yellow square back into the leg slot. Compare the yellow parallelogram with the corresponding red rectangle inside the hypotenuse square.
- "Now we are going to look at the red rectangle and the yellow parallelogram. I will put the square back and turn the frame so we still have a good workspace. We will look at the base of the parallelogram and the base of the rectangle. We check to see whether the bases are the same. They are. This base is equal to this base, so the bases are equivalent."
Slide the red rectangle into the testing channel, then slide the yellow parallelogram in next to it. Observe their height alignment.
- "Now we check the height. I place the red rectangle first. Its height matches this rectangle. Then I place the parallelogram. The parallelogram, oriented this way, has exactly the same height."
- "Therefore, the red rectangle is equivalent to the yellow parallelogram. Since we already proved that the yellow parallelogram is equivalent to the yellow square, we can say that the red rectangle is equivalent to the yellow square. All three pieces are equivalent to one another."

Presentation IV: Equivalence of the Blue Square

Retrieve the blue square (from its side storage) and the blue parallelogram from the bottom sliding track. Align their bases.
- "Now let’s return these to the plate and look at the blue square and the blue parallelogram. First, we check whether the bases are the same. They are. It may not have appeared that way at first, but once we align them, we can see that the bases are equal."
Slide the blue square and the blue parallelogram into the testing slot to verify their identical heights.
- "Next, we check the height. I turn the frame and place the blue square. It has the same height as this rectangle. Then I place the parallelogram. It also has the same height. The height of the square and the height of the parallelogram are exactly the same."
Compare the blue parallelogram to the other red rectangle division of the hypotenuse square.
- "Now we will compare the blue parallelogram with the red rectangle. First, we check the base. The base of the red rectangle and the base of the blue parallelogram are equal. Now we check the height. I slide these over and use the rectangle to check the height."
- "I place the red rectangle first. Its height matches this rectangle. Then I place the parallelogram. Its height, when oriented this way, is the same as the red rectangle."
- "If two figures have the same base and the same height, they are equivalent. Therefore, the red rectangle is equivalent to the blue parallelogram. We also know that the blue square is equivalent to the blue parallelogram. So the red rectangle is equivalent to the blue parallelogram, which is equivalent to the blue square. We can say that the red rectangle is equivalent to the blue square."

Presentation V: Geometrical Synthesis & Projections

Point to the red square as a whole, highlighting the dividing height projection line.
- "When we look at the inset in the middle, our red square is made up of the two rectangles we have been discussing."
- "The length of the longest side of our smaller red rectangle is the same as the length of the hypotenuse, because this is a square. Our hypotenuse forms one side, and all the sides of a square are equal. So the long side of our smaller red rectangle is equal to the length of the hypotenuse."
Perform a sliding gesture from the short leg of the triangle down onto the hypotenuse plane.
- "In geometry, there is a way of thinking about the length of the smaller side, the small leg. To think in that way, we use our imagination."
- "If we look at this leg of the right angle triangle and imagine sliding it down, what we would create is equivalent to the short side of the smaller red rectangle. That is called the projection of the leg. If we slid this leg down, we would create this short side."
- "The same is true for the other leg. If we slid that leg down, we would create the short side of the larger red rectangle."
- "We know that one side of the larger red rectangle is equal to the hypotenuse, and the short side is the projection of that leg onto the hypotenuse."
- "This demonstrates what Euclid said: in a right angled triangle, the square on one leg is equivalent to the rectangle built from the hypotenuse and the projection of that leg onto the hypotenuse."

"Euclid’s plate is an extension of what we know about Pythagoras. Euclid had a number of theorems. In the presentations we just did, we are not proving the theorems formally. What we are doing is a sensorial exploration. To prove a theorem requires precise mathematical calculation."

"All of this work is based in logic. It is food for the reasoning mind. The work we did with Euclid’s theorem and the Euclid plate is a syllogism. A syllogism means that if $A = B$ and $B = C$, then $A = C$."

Adult Theoretical Guide (For Teacher Understanding Only)

If we label one leg of the right triangle as $A$, the other leg as $B$, and the hypotenuse as $C$. Then, let $P$ represent the projection segment of leg $A$ onto the hypotenuse, and let $Q$ represent the projection segment of leg $B$ onto the hypotenuse. We have mathematically divided the hypotenuse into two segments such that $C = P + Q$.

Euclid’s projection theorem proves that: $$A^2 = P \times C$$ $$B^2 = Q \times C$$ This supports the logical reasoning that the square built on a leg ($A^2$ or $B^2$) is equivalent to the area of the rectangle formed by the hypotenuse ($C$) and the leg's projection segment ($P$ or $Q$).
