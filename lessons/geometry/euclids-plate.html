Geometry // Further Explorations with Equivalence

Euclid's Plate

Guide: Elim Lee // AMI Elementary Submission

Direct Aim

To explore Euclidean geometry sensorially and begin to reason through geometric proofs.

Materials

Euclid’s Plate material (frame, rectangles, squares, parallelograms, white triangle)

Presentation

Invite the child and introduce Euclid as the author of "The Elements."
"Euclid lived around 300 BCE and wrote ten axioms—things everyone accepts as true. Let’s see if we can prove Pythagoras’ theorem using this plate."
Illustration: Initial setup of Euclid's Plate with all insets in place.
Remove the red rectangles. Slide the white triangle to the bottom of the frame.
"I’ll move the blue and yellow parallelograms into this empty space. Notice that they are equivalent to the red square."
Illustration: Triangle at bottom; parallelograms filling the red square space.
Reset the plate. Focus on the yellow square and parallelogram.
"I’ll remove the yellow square and slide the white triangle up. The yellow parallelogram fits perfectly. They are equivalent."
Repeat for the blue square and blue parallelogram.
"We have shown that the two smaller squares equal the two parallelograms, and the two parallelograms equal the red square. Pythagoras is proven!"
Illustration: Showing the equivalence of the blue shapes.

The Reasoning Level (Syllogism)

Compare the bases and heights of the yellow square and parallelogram.
"If two figures have the same base and the same height, they are equivalent. Now let’s compare the yellow parallelogram to the small red rectangle."
Demonstrate that the red rectangle and yellow parallelogram share the same base and height.
"Since A=B and B=C, then A=C. The yellow square is equivalent to the red rectangle."
Illustration: Measuring bases and heights against the frame.
Summarize using Euclid’s theorem of projections.
"The square on one leg is equivalent to the rectangle built from the hypotenuse and the projection of that leg onto the hypotenuse."
Illustration: Diagram showing the 'projection' line from the triangle height.
