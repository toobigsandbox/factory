Geometry // Further Explorations with Equivalence

Euclid's Plate

Guide: Elim Lee // AMI Elementary Submission

Direct Aim

To explore Euclidean geometry sensorially and begin to reason through geometric proofs of the Pythagorean theorem.

Materials

Euclid’s Plate frame, red squares/rectangles, blue/yellow parallelograms, white triangle.

Presentation I: The Initial Setup

Invite the child and introduce Euclid as the author of "The Elements."
"Euclid lived around 300 BCE and wrote axioms—things everyone accepts as true. Let’s see if we can prove Pythagoras’ theorem using this plate."
Fig 1.1: Initial Layout showing squares on legs and hypotenuse

Presentation II: Sensorial Proof

Remove the red rectangles from the frame. Slide the white triangle to the bottom corner.
"I’ll move the blue and yellow parallelograms into this empty space. Notice that they fill it perfectly."
Fig 2.1: Parallelograms demonstrating equivalence to the squares

The Reasoning Level (Syllogism)

Compare the base and height of the yellow square to the yellow parallelogram.
"If two figures have the same base and the same height, they are equivalent. Let's measure them."
Fig 3.1: Proving equivalence through base and height
Summarize using Euclid’s theorem of projections.
"Since A is equivalent to B, and B is equivalent to C, then A must be equivalent to C. The square on the leg is equivalent to the rectangle on the hypotenuse."
Fig 4.1: The Theorem of Projections
