Geometry // Further Explorations with Equivalence

Euclid's Plate: Sensorial Introduction

Guide: Elim Lee // AMI Elementary Submission

Material Analysis

Euclid's Plate: A structural frame featuring a central inset space for a white right triangle, flanked by a large red square on the hypotenuse (divided into two rectangles), a blue square on the vertical leg, a yellow square on the horizontal leg, and custom matching blue and yellow equivalent parallelograms stored in slide tracks on the left side of the board.

Purpose

To sensorially demonstrate equivalence of squares and parallelograms on Euclid's Plate, proving Pythagoras' Theorem through direct visual and physical manipulation.

Presentation I: Historical Narrative

Gather the children on the rug. Begin telling the story of Euclid's mathematical influence in a calm, narrative tone.
- "You might remember that we talked about Euclid when we heard the story of geometry, and you may remember that I mentioned that our former president, Abraham Lincoln, read Euclid’s book to train his own mind."
- "Hardly anything is really known about Euclid. We know that he lived around 300 BCE, but we do not know exactly when he was born or when he died, or much else about his life, other than that he wrote extensively about mathematics. He wrote about arithmetic, but he is most famous for writing about another branch of mathematics: geometry."
- "One of the things he is well known for is his book The Elements. You can still find that book in bookstores today. He wrote ten axioms in mathematics. An axiom is something that everyone simply accepts as being true. It is something that makes people ponder."
- "For example, Euclid said that if you have two points, there is only one straight line between them. You might like to think about that for a moment. Does it seem true?"
- "Euclid’s book was so influential that it was used as a geometry textbook until the 1600s CE. That is a very long time ago. Even today, people still study the book. The type of geometry we study is called Euclidean geometry. There are other types of geometry as well, but those are stories for another day."

Presentation II: Introducing the Plate & First Equivalence Verification

Place Euclid's Plate carefully onto the mat. Trace the square spaces surrounding the triangle.
- "Now let’s look at this plate. It looks a little familiar, especially here. It looks very much like our plate of Pythagoras. Let’s see if we can prove Pythagoras’ theorem here."
- "What does Pythagoras state? That the square built on one leg of a right triangle, added to the square built on the other leg, equals the square built on the hypotenuse."
- "Let’s see if we can show that using the parallelograms."
Gently lift the two red rectangles surrounding the red hypotenuse square and set them aside. Slide the white triangle all the way down to the bottom of its niche.
- "The first thing I am going to do is remove the red rectangles. Then I will slide the white triangle down to the bottom, and I will move the parallelograms into this space."
Pick up the blue and yellow parallelograms and arrange them in the space originally occupied by the red hypotenuse square.
- "I will move one here and the yellow one here."
- "What do you notice?"
- "You may notice that the red square is equal to the blue parallelogram plus the yellow parallelogram. They are equivalent. The blue parallelogram plus the yellow parallelogram equals the red square."
- "But that is not quite what we are asking, is it?"
Carefully return the blue and yellow parallelograms to their side slots. Slide the white triangle back up to its native position, and place the red rectangles back in the bottom of the frame.
- "So I will remove the parallelograms and put them back. I will slide the white triangle back up and replace the red rectangles."

Presentation III: Proving Individual Equivalent Relationships

Isolate the yellow square and the corresponding yellow parallelogram.
- "Now let’s focus on the yellow square and the yellow parallelogram. I will remove the yellow square. The yellow square took up this space right here."
Remove the yellow square and lay it on the mat. Slide the white right triangle upwards into the empty space.
- "I am going to slide the white triangle up into that space. It is the same amount of space. I have not removed anything else."
Take the yellow parallelogram and slide it into the empty space left behind by the shifted pieces.
- "Let’s see what happens. The yellow parallelogram fits right here. It takes up the same amount of space as the yellow square."
- "What can we say about the yellow square and the yellow parallelogram? They take up the same amount of space. Using a mathematical term, we say they are equivalent."
Reset the yellow square, yellow parallelogram, and slide the white triangle back to its baseline position. Focus on the blue elements.
- "Now let’s look at the blue square and the blue parallelogram. Do they take up the same amount of space? It may not look like it at first, but let’s see what happens."
Remove the blue square and place it on the mat. Slide the white triangle sideways into the blue square's native space.
- "I will remove the blue square and slide the triangle. I have not added anything. Now watch."
Slide the equivalent blue parallelogram into the remaining open slot on the plate.
- "The blue parallelogram takes up the same amount of space as the blue square. What can we say about these two figures, the blue square and the blue parallelogram? We can say that they are equivalent."

Presentation IV: Synthesis

Point sequentially to each segment of the plate to gather the final proofs together.
- "What we have seen is this: the red square is equivalent to the blue parallelogram plus the yellow parallelogram."
- "We have shown that the yellow square is equivalent to the yellow parallelogram, and that the blue square is equivalent to the blue parallelogram."
- "That means that the two parallelograms added together equal the red square, and that the two squares added together equal the red square. It is exactly what Pythagoras stated."
- "Here is the remarkable part: it does not matter how small or how large our right triangle is. You can still construct squares and parallelograms that are equivalent."
- "You might like to try it."
