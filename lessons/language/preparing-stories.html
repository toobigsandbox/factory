Preparing to Tell a Story

Guide Name: Elim Lee | AMI Elementary Curriculum

Direct Aim / Purpose

The direct aim of this lesson is to provide a comprehensive guide for adults on how to effectively prepare and tell a story to children or groups, emphasizing storytelling as a foundational part of the elementary experience.

Materials
  • The selected story
  • Necessary props, pictures, or items
  • Water (to prevent coughing or thirst during delivery)
  • Cue cards (optional, for staying on track)
  • A storytelling log (optional, for post-story reflection)

Presentation

01.

Invite the adult student or gather storytelling materials. Introduce the core value of narrative in the Elementary Montessori classroom.

“Storytelling is a major part of the elementary experience. Here’s some information to help you, the adult, prepare yourself to give the story to a child or group. This presentation is for you, not directly for the children. However, if a child shows interest or asks, you can help them become a better storyteller by sharing this presentation with them. We will go through five steps to prepare your story.”

02.

Detail the five critical phases of narrative preparation, highlighting how variation theory adapts the process to individual sensory learning styles.

“Step one: Identify the story you’re going to tell.”

“Step two: Read that story several times. The first time, read it for pleasure—find joy or a personal connection with the story; that’s very important.”

“Step two B: Read from the perspective of the author, the characters involved, the time, culture, and daily life.”

“Step three: Practice the practicalities—read the story aloud, time yourself, and adjust to the needs of your audience. Include any actions, props, pictures, or items you may need to share.”

“Step four: Outline the story according to your personal learning style. At minimum, have a beginning, middle, and end.”

“Step five: Make the story your own. This is very important. You can do this in three ways:”

“A) Make the story come alive to you by telling it as if you know it personally; this creates a deeper connection with your students.”

“B) Use your senses to internalize the story. If you’re an auditory learner, tape record yourself. If you’re kinesthetic, write out your outline or key parts. If you’re visual, use pictures or images to help recall the story.”

“C) Use incidents in the story as landmarks to help you progress through the narrative.”

“After preparing in these ways, practice telling the story. These five steps will help you prepare to give your story effectively. Next, we’ll talk about actually telling the story with your audience.”

03.

Prepare your storytelling space ahead of time by ensuring comfort for yourself and visibility for your audience. Arrange any props or materials you will use.

“Step three in telling the story is preparing your environment. You are part of that environment, so prepare yourself physically. Use the bathroom beforehand, have water available in case you cough or get thirsty, and wear comfortable clothing and shoes. Know where you will stand or sit. Make sure there’s space for your audience and that they can see you clearly. If needed, prepare a few Q cards to help you stay on track.”

04.

Smile warmly and make eye contact to invite children into the story.

“When you begin telling the story, smile and make everyone feel welcome. Invite them into the experience with you. Introduce the story by name and tell them you will take questions and comments at the end. You can start with something like, 'A long, long time ago...'—this is a great way to begin stories, especially historical ones.”

05.

Use landmarks or notes discreetly to help your delivery. Adjust your tone, volume, and pace to keep children engaged.

“As the story unfolds, engage your audience with your eyes and voice. Use your prepared landmarks to move the story along. Remember, you are the keeper of the story. If you get lost, you can use your landmarks to reconnect and adjust the story as needed to keep it engaging and on track. Your audience won’t know if you make slight changes, even if they’ve heard the story before. Remind your audience to hold their comments until the end.”

06.

Deliver the story resolution clearly and adapt dynamic timing based on sensory feedback from the circle.

“As you come to the end of your story, if you sense your audience losing interest, adjust and wrap up quickly but be sure to give the resolution or key information. Bring the story to a clear ending. Do not ask questions at the end, though the audience may ask you questions.”

07.

After finishing, pause and allow for audience questions and comments.

“After the storytelling session, take time to reflect on how it went. Think about what you liked and what you would change for next time. If you keep a storytelling log, write down your reflections and observations to help you improve.”

Instructor's Notes // Pedagogical Aside

"The Keeper of the Story": If you lose your place or the audience's attention wanes, adjust the content fluidly to maintain engagement and flow; the audience will not notice the shifts.

Extending to the Child: While this presentation is primarily prepared for the adult guide, if a child shows a deep interest, passion, or innate talent in storytelling, support them! Share these exact five preparation steps to scaffold and develop their expressive storytelling skills.

Post-Story Boundaries: Never ask the audience questions at the end of the storytelling. Allow children to sit in reflection and initiate their own questions, comments, or independent work when they are ready.

Storytelling Log: It is highly recommended to maintain a personal story-giving log. Track what stories were told, which landmarks succeeded, environmental adjustments, and general observations to ensure continuous improvement as a keeper of history, science, and literature.
