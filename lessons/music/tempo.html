Music // Time & Speed

Tempo

Guide: Elim Lee // AMI Elementary Submission

Purpose

To provide an opportunity for the child to understand tempo through whole‑body movement, singing, and instrument playing.

Materials

Tone bars, Mallets.
Tempo Cards: adagio (slow), allegro (fast), andante (walking), accelerando (becoming faster), ritardando (becoming slower).

Presentation

Gather the children in an open space and introduce the concept of the musical "heartbeat."
"Every piece of music has a heartbeat—a speed that it moves at. In music, we use the word 'Tempo' to describe this speed."
Place the Andante card on the mat and strike a steady beat on a tone bar.
"Andante is a walking pace. Let’s walk around the room to this beat. Keep your steps exactly with my strikes."
Wait for the children to find the rhythm, then stop and switch to the Allegro card.
"Allegro means happy and fast! Now our heartbeat is moving quickly. Can you move your feet to this happy speed?"
Switch to the Adagio card and play very slow, resonant strikes.
"Now we move to Adagio. It is slow and steady. We must exert great control over our bodies to move this slowly."

Transitions of Speed

Introduce the Accelerando card. Begin slowly and gradually increase the speed of the tone bar.
"Sometimes the music starts to get faster and faster. This is called Accelerando. Can you feel your walking start to speed up with me?"
Introduce the Ritardando card. Begin at a moderate pace and gradually slow down.
"And sometimes, the music wants to rest. It gets slower and slower. We call this Ritardando."
"Tempo is one of the most sensorial elements of music. For the second-plane child, the challenge is not just the speed, but the coordination required to transition smoothly between them. Encourage them to find these terms in their favorite recordings."
Elim Lee // April 1st Sprint // AMI Elementary
