Music // Musical Expression

Details of Music Writing

Guide: Elim Lee // April 1st Sprint // AMI Elementary

Purpose

To introduce musical expression symbols, giving children the language and control to interpret music expressively. Once introduced, children can apply these details to known songs and use them in their own composition work.

Materials List

  • Three Concept Cards: Simple, clean, mounted, and laminated cards depicting Legato, Staccato, and Fermata.

The Concept Cards Overview

Legato

  • Definition: Smoothly connected notes without interruption.
  • Symbol: Curved line (like a slur).
  • Effect: One note flows directly into the next.

Staccato

  • Definition: Short notes that are interrupted or detached.
  • Symbol: Dot above or below a note.
  • Effect: Each note is separated with space or a stop.

Fermata

  • Definition: To pause or hold the note longer than usual.
  • Symbol: Arc with a dot underneath (like an eye).
  • Effect: You hold the note… and then come in together again.

Presentation I: Introducing the Concepts

Guide Tip: Introduce all three symbols at the same time. They contrast nicely, and it gives the children an instant sense of how music can change in feeling just by how it’s played or sung. You don’t have to introduce the nomenclature first—let them experience the expression through movement and voice, then connect the experience to the name and symbol.
Show the three prepared cards to the children.
"We have learned how to write the names of the notes. Today, we are going to learn how to write the *feeling* of the notes."
Fig 1.1: The three expression symbols prepared on simple, laminated cards.

Presentation II: Demonstration (Hot Cross Buns)

Introduce the Legato concept using a familiar song.
"When you see this symbol (legato), it means the notes are connected together. Almost slurred—like you don’t hear any spaces in between. One note flows right into the next."
Sing "Hot Cross Buns" with legato phrasing, letting the notes blend smoothly.
"🎵 One a penny, two a penny, hot cross buns... 🎵"
Introduce the Fermata concept.
"When we see a fermata, we pause and hold that note. I’ll put my hand out like this [extends hand in front] when it’s the one with the fermata."
Fig 2.1: Applying the Fermata hold to a familiar lyric.
Sing "Hot Cross Buns" with an exaggerated hold on the final note. Extend your hand as the visual cue.
"🎵 Hot cross [hold] buns... 🎵
🎵 One a penny, two a penny, hot cross [hold] buns... 🎵"
Implementation Note: It’s tricky when it’s just one person demonstrating, but imagine this with the whole group holding together, and then all coming in at once. The children love singing familiar songs with these new styles—especially with long holds where everyone has to listen and come in together!
Pro Tip Even if it seems repetitive to us, repetition is how children integrate expression. They will want to do it again and again—so let them. Yes, you can use Hot Cross Buns for all of this. It’s short, familiar, and easy to modify.
