Simple Songs

Guide: Elim Lee // AMI Elementary Submission

Purpose

  • To provide opportunities for exploration and practice using the major scale in playing simple, recognizable songs.
  • To strengthen the child’s ear–hand connection in music-making.
  • To foster joy, confidence, and independence through successful music experiences.
  • To connect sensorial exploration (tone bars, scale) with musical literacy (numbers, notation).

Material Analysis

  • Tone Bars: A full diatonic set of 8 tuned metal bars representing the $C$ Major scale (C, D, E, F, G, A, B, C), mounted on separate wooden blocks.
  • Rubber Mallet: A specialized striker with a soft, circular rubber head to produce a warm, rich, and lingering resonance.
  • Major Scale Pattern Strip: A white guide strip labeled with the scale degree numbers $1$ to $8$ aligned underneath the bars.
  • Song Cards Envelope: A stored packet containing individual cards with familiar children's melodies represented solely by simple sequences of numbers (e.g., $3 - 2 - 1$).

Presentation

**The Guide invites the child to the Music shelves and introduces the Tone Bars and the Simple Songs Envelope.**

"Now that we know how to use this strip with our tone bars—and how to make our tone bar sound beautiful—we can learn how to play a few simple songs."

**The Guide removes the envelope labeled 'Simple Songs' from the shelf and holds it before the child.**

"On our shelf, we have this envelope, and it has simple songs in it. They’re written using numbers, so we’re going to choose one. I will choose this song."

**The Guide pulls out the selected song card, places it flat on the table where it is clearly visible, and pulls down the respective tone bars corresponding to the scale pattern.**

"What I’m going to do is place this where I can see it and read it. So, there it is, right there. I’ll just leave my strip right here and pull down my tone bars."

**The Guide strikes the starting tonic bar gently to test and ensure the setup is accurate and fully in tune.**

"Let me just make sure I’ve done it correctly... [Plays tone bar 1]... Sounds like it!"

**The Guide looks deliberately at the numbered song card, model reading, and strikes the sequence of tone bars beautifully using the mallet.**

"Shall I read and play? [Plays melody for 'Hot Cross Buns' sequentially]... That’s Hot Cross Buns, isn’t it?"

**The Guide slides the major scale pattern strip to start on a different bar, preparing the child for transposition without using confusing jargon.**

"Let’s move our strip. I’ll put the tone bars back. Now, move the strip anywhere you want to, and we’ll see if it still sounds like Hot Cross Buns."

**The Guide plays the exact same number sequence starting on the new base key, illustrating that the melody stays identical despite the pitch shift.**

"Does that sound like Hot Cross Buns? Yeah—it does! You can move the strip anywhere you want, and play a song. Then move the strip again. Does the song still sound the same? We have a whole envelope full of songs, and we have so many bars—any of them could be your starting or 'Do' bar. Have fun!"

Instructor's Notes & Exploration

  • Modeling Reading Behavior: It is crucial that the Guide models reading the numbers card as they strike the corresponding tone bars. Do not perform the song merely from physical memory; turn your gaze directly onto the card.
  • Creative Lyrical Freedom: At this stage, children are highly encouraged to compose their own simple melodies using numbers alone before receiving traditional musical notation.
  • Story of BM & Phantom of the Opera: If the children demonstrate extreme curiosity about key signatures or changing from Major to Minor scales, recall the fascinating story of BM’s choice!
