Music // Tone Bars: Major Scales

Degrees of the Scale

Exploring Tonal Roles through the Royal Court of C // Guide: Elim Lee

Direct Aims & Purpose

To introduce the technical names of the degrees of the scale; to help children understand their functional, relational roles within music; to recognize them systematically in order and position; and to begin to internalize their musical relationships.

Material Analysis

Full tone bar set (25 bars, C4 to C6) mounted adjacent on wooden boards, a standardized yellow major scale transposer strip, a rubber-headed striker, two physical miniature decorative crowns, and dual-sided degree tickets (Word/Name on front, Number on reverse).

DEGREE TICKETS:
  • Tonic (1st)
  • Supertonic (2nd)
  • Mediant (3rd)
  • Subdominant (4th)
 
  • Dominant (5th)
  • Submediant (6th)
  • Leading Tone (7th)
  • Tonic (8th)

Court Role

Presentation

Invite a small group of children to the tone bar cabinet. Arrange the diatonic set of tone bars.
"We have spent time matching our notes, and we've learned their names. Today, we're going to see how each note has a special role—a job of its own in our musical home."
Place a crown over the first C4 tone bar, and a second crown over the high C5 tone bar.
"I have a story to tell you today. This story is called the Royal Court of the Kingdom of C. Once upon a time, there was a kingdom that was exactly eight miles across. The boundaries of this kingdom were ruled by a King and a Queen. The King lived at the lower boundary, the first note of our scale. The Queen lived at the highest boundary, the eighth note of our scale."

Introduce the first council assistant, the Lord High Chamberlain at the 5th degree. Place the ticket.
"The King decided he needed some help, so he hired a Lord High Chamberlain. He placed him at the fifth degree—our Dominant note. He sits right in the middle of the kingdom, watching over both sides. But because he was a bit far for the King to hear clearly, the King hired a messenger to live at the third degree—the Mediant."
Introduce the Queen's council assistants. Place the Subdominant (4th) and Submediant (6th) tickets.
"Seeing this, the Queen decided to form her own counsel! She hired her own Chamberlain at the fourth degree—the Subdominant. And she also hired her own messenger to live at the sixth degree—the Submediant."
Introduce the final two court members. Place the Leading Tone (7th) and Supertonic (2nd) tickets.
"There are two other members of the court. The first is the Queen’s Lady-in-Waiting, who lives at the seventh degree. She is very shy and never goes anywhere without the Queen. Listen to how closely she leans toward the Queen's home... she is the Leading Tone. And finally, the King's Footman, who rides just above the King in his royal coach—living at the second degree, the Supertonic."
Strike each tone bar in sequence as you point to the newly placed tickets. Let the story hang in the space.
"Now, whenever any musical traveler enters the Kingdom, they must bow to the King and Queen before they begin and before they leave. Would you like to play these notes and tell the story of the Kingdom of C yourself?"

Interdisciplinary Connections & Color Wheels

Language Connection: The prefixes super- (above) and sub- (below) are beautifully explored here. The Supertonic sits immediately above the Tonic, while the Subdominant is the identical interval (a fifth) below the upper Tonic.

Art Connection (Montessori Degrees): The tonal degrees reflect the structural colors of the color wheel. The King’s foundational framework (1st, 3rd, 5th) represents the primary triad (red, yellow, blue), while the Queen's assistants (4th and 6th) reflect the secondary colors (green, indigo/purple).
