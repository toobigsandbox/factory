Music // The Tone Bars

Tone Bars (3): Sharps & Flats

Guide: Elim Lee // AMI Elementary Submission

Purpose

To introduce the nomenclature and notation of accidental notes (sharps and flats), emphasizing the relative nature of musical pitch.

Materials

  • Tone Bars: Set including black and white holders.
  • Black Moveable Notes: Discs with sharps on one side, flats on the reverse.
  • Staff Board: Green board with G and F clefs.
  • Wooden Symbols: Sharp (#), Flat (b), and Natural.

Presentation I: Naming

Play the C Major scale. Label each white bar with its name disc.
"We have named the white tone bars. Today, I want to show you something new. We are going to name these other tone bars in the octave."
Show the wooden Sharp (#) sign. Play 'A', then play the black bar to the right.
"A sharp means just a little bit higher. If we play 'A', and then move one half-step higher, we call this 'A-Sharp'."
Fig 3.1: Identifying a Sharp through relative pitch
Show the wooden Flat (b) sign. Play 'B', then play the same black bar.
"A flat means just a little bit lower. If we play 'B', and move one half-step lower, we have 'B-Flat'. This bar has two names!"
Flip the black nomenclature disc from A# to Bb.
"One sound, but two names depending on which way we are traveling."

Presentation II: Notation on the Staff

Place the green staff board near the bars. Place the G-clef.
"If we want to write A-Sharp, we place the sharp symbol next to our note on the second space. But if we call it B-Flat, we write it on the third line with a flat symbol."
Fig 3.2: Same sound, different notation (Enharmonics)
"Note to the Guide: This discovery often surprises children. Remind them that sharps and flats are relative: a half-step to the right is a sharp, and a half-step to the left is a flat. Later, you may introduce the Natural sign to negate these."
Elim Lee // Semester 4 Sprint // AMI Elementary
