Music // Tone Bars: Naming & Notation

Naming & Notation

Prepared by: Elim Lee // AMI Elementary Submission

Purpose

  • To introduce the letter names of the tone bars ($C_4$ through $C_6$) and connect sound directly to symbol.
  • To illustrate major diatonic scales and their written properties.
  • To provide an elegant transition from the sensory Bells materials of the first plane to the abstract, mathematical music reading system of the second plane.
  • To build cognitive pattern-recognition pathways that align musical transposition with mathematical translation rules.

Material Analysis

  • Chromatic Tone Bars: A full set of $25$ chromatic bars spanning two complete octaves from middle $C_4$ to high $C_6$. Natural keys are encased in flat white surfaces; chromatic accidentals are encased in flat black surfaces. Centered on each bar is an exposed silver strike-plate.
  • Tone Bar Resonator Boards: Two joined boards painted to resemble a piano keyboard, maintaining a fixed workspace.
  • Moveable Disks: Flat, circular wooden tags featuring note letter names ($c, d, e, \dots$) and enharmonic sharp/flat names ($a^\sharp / b^\flat$).
  • Major Scale Transposer Strip: A laminated strip displaying step-intervals numbered $1$ to $8$ matching the whole/half step progression.

Presentation I: Transition from the Bells

The Guide assembles a small group of children near the music cabinet, presenting the bells and tone bars.
"You already know how the bells sing from the lowest step to the highest step. Today we are going to invite these new bells—our tone bars—to show us how we write those steps down on paper."
The Guide plays up and down the diatonic bells octave, then places a wooden name disk on each corresponding bell.
"Let's sing the notes as I play: C, D, E, F, G, A, B, C."
The Guide aligns the transposer strip underneath the C Major tone bars on the board, pulling down the keys matching steps 1 through 8.
"Look at these tone bars that are aligned with our numbers. Let's play the first bar. [Strikes $C_4$ bell, then $C_4$ tone bar]. They sound exactly the same!"
The Guide moves the wooden disk from the $C$ bell to the $C$ tone bar, repeating the process for the entire octave.
"From here on out, we will work with these beautiful tone bars. We can return our bells to the primary classroom."
Fig 1.2: Casing & silver strike plate alignment on transposer strip

Presentation II: Sliding the Strip (Octaves)

The Guide points to the natural octave bars now clearly labeled with note names.
"But wait—there is more! We have many more tone bars on our board. Let me show you what we can do with this strip."
The Guide places a finger on step 8 (high C) and slides the transposer strip to the right, so step 1 now aligns with that exact bar.
"Because this is a C, and this is also a C, I can place my number 1 right where my number 8 was. I'll slide my strip right here... [Slides the strip to C5]."
The Guide pulls down the new octave tone bars matching steps 2 through 8 of the next register.
"Now we can continue notating our higher octave! C, D, E... let's find the rest of our white bars."

Naming Accidentals & Staff Rules

When presenting accidentals (sharps and flats), the Guide introduces the relative terms: **"a half step higher"** (sharps) and **"a half step lower"** (flats).

Rules for Written Note Names:

  • The accidental symbol is a superscript when written as a word: e.g., $F^\sharp$, $B^\flat$.
  • The scale name letter is always **CAPITALIZED** (e.g., "G Major").
  • Individual notes notated on paper are **lowercase** (e.g., "this is note g").

Rules for Staff Placement:

  • On the staff, the accidental sign comes **FIRST** (to the left of the note head) so the musician reads it *before* playing.
  • Note Stem Rule: If the note head is above the middle staff line ($B_4$), the stem extends down and to the left. If below the middle line, the stem goes up and to the right. On the middle line, it can go either way with intention.

Cognitive Link to State Math Standards: Pattern building and algebraic replacement are foundational components of elementary curricula. When children substitute letter names with abstract variables ($a$, $b$) or learn transposition ratios, they are actively participating in mathematical transformation work. Keep this connection at the forefront during parent-guide reviews.
