Music // The Tone Bars

Sensorial Introduction to the Tone Bars

Guide: Elim Lee // April 1st Sprint // AMI Elementary

Material Overview

Construction: The tone bars are evenly wide blocks, painted black and white to mimic the piano keyboard. Unlike a piano, the black keys are not indented; they sit flush in the layout.

The Playing Mechanism: To play a note, use two fingers to tug the intended tone bar forward by its plastic casing. Once pulled out, tap the silver center of the bar with the mallet.

The Pattern Strip: A "Transposer" made of a slip of poster paper. It features Sharpie-written numbers (1-8) with deliberately blank squares in between to emphasize Whole Steps.

Purpose

To introduce the tone bars as a sensorial tool for pitch refinement and to discover the mathematical pattern of the major scale using the transposer strip.

Materials

  • 25 Tone Bars: Arranged chromatically on two boards.
  • Mallet: With a hard rubber head.
  • Major Scale Pattern Strip: Numbered 1-8 with whole-step gaps.

Part I: Matching and Grading

Invite the children to the rug. Set out two sets of white tone bars in random order.
"You have worked with the bells to find twin sounds. The tone bars work the same way. We are going to find the sounds that match exactly."
Strike a control bar, then search for its match in the second set.
"These two sounds are twins. They belong together."
Invite the child to arrange one set from the deepest pitch to the highest.
"Now we have a staircase of sound, climbing from low to high."

Part II: The Major Scale & The Transposer

"Guide Tip: When striking the bars, remember the principle: 'Never Louder Than Lovely.' Strike with a top-down motion and allow the mallet to bounce back immediately to let the silver center vibrate freely."
Place the bars in keyboard order. Slide the pattern strip underneath the bars.
"Every major scale follows a secret code. We use this numbered strip to find the notes for any scale, starting anywhere we like."
Place the strip starting on 'C'. Use two fingers to tug the bars numbered 1 through 8 forward.
"Watch as I pull out the bars the pattern has chosen for us. Now we play only these."
Fig 1.2: Pattern strip showing "Touch" (Half Step) vs "Blank" (Whole Step)
Strike the silver center of the pulled-out bars in sequence.
"When we start on C, the pattern lands only on white bars. But if we move the strip to start on G, the pattern will tell us to use a black bar. The pattern is the law."
"The strip acts as a bridge between the physical distance of the bars and the mathematical logic of the scale. Ensure the child uses a steady pincer grip on the mallet to produce a clear, sustained ring."
