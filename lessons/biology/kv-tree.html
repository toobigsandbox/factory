Biology - Classification

KV Tree Classification

Guide Elim Lee

Purpose

To collaboratively build a tree of classification for Kingdom Vegetalia, engage in physical movement with the materials, and connect classification levels from kingdom down to family.

Materials List

  • A large floor or table space.
  • Kingdom Vegetalia classification circles.
  • Kingdom branches (from chart box/large folder).
  • Blank classification circles (white or pre-printed).
  • Colored pencils matching paper border colors.
  • Calligraphy pens or high-quality writing tools.

Presentation

Step 1: Prepare the collaborative workspace. Prepare a large space for the activity. Gather all classification circles and branches from folders, placing them ready for group work. Organize branches in a chart box or designated folder labeled “Kingdom branches.” Encourage multiple children to work together to manage the materials and sorting.

"Okay, so we're going to do one more thing with Kingdom Vegetalia, and this is the tree of classification. Now, we need a big space for this. I’ve pulled all the circles from all of the folders, and we also have some branches... You won’t be doing this with just two hands; you’ll have many hands working alongside you because children will be working in groups according to their characteristics and experience."

Step 2: Place the primary Kingdom and division indicators. Place the largest Kingdom Vegetalia circle on the floor or table. Add the branch representing the division (Pteridophyte or Cryptogam) next to it. Encourage students to recall and name the two divisions.

"I’m going to take this circle and place this branch here. We know that in our material, Kingdom Vegetalia is divided into two different divisions. What are they? Yes, it’s division Pteridophyte, where we can see how the plant reproduces, and Cryptogam, the division where it keeps it secret."
Illustration: Classification Tree Setup

Step 3: Connect Angiosperms, Gymnosperms, and subclasses. Add the branches for Angiosperm and Gymnosperm to the tree. Place circles representing these two groups under their respective branches. Then, add the branch labeled Dicot and show its two subclasses: Metachider and Archo. Lay out family circles under each subclass.

"If we think about these plants and how they reproduce, scientists have divided them into two groups... What are those two groups? Exactly right! We have the branch Angiosperm—the plants that have their seeds inside a vessel—and Gymnosperm, those plants with naked seeds... Let’s think now about Angiosperm. We know Angiosperm is further divided into two different classes... Yes, you are absolutely right. We have the Dicot class and the Monocot class. Let’s think about Dicot right now. We know there are two subclasses for Dicot. What are those two subclasses? Yes, we have Metachider (recent) and Archo (ancient)."

Step 4: Distribute family circles and split the Monocot division. Assist children in placing family-level circles under the appropriate subclasses. Encourage discussion of family features like flower types, stem shapes, or other distinctive traits. Add the monocot branch and divide it into its two groups, placing corresponding circles beneath. Walk around to assist and keep the material organized.

"Now, for the family level, you’ll continue organizing each branch and lay them out. For example, what do you remember about the families for Metachider? Oh yes, the sunflowers—those composite flowers we really like. Right, and the mints with the square stem... But it’s not just Dicot that has subclasses. Monocot does too. What are the further divisions here? We have monocot group one and group two."

Step 5: Synthesize and reflect on future tree growth. Present the completed classification tree for Kingdom Vegetalia with all branches and circles laid out. Encourage children to visualize expanding the tree further as they learn more.

"And here we are with our tree—a tree of classification. Look how many more places there are where our tree can grow. You have so much information, knowledge, and experience that I know you can tend to the growing of our beautiful tree."

Instructor's Pedagogical Aside

  • Pedagogical Approach: This work should be presented as a gentle invitation rather than a demanding assignment, empowering children to feel free to explore rather than obligated.
  • Preparation Warning: Do not laminate the blank circles or materials meant for the children; they need to be able to write and draw directly on them.
  • Cross-Curricular Skills: This activity is a perfect invitation for children to practice their very best handwriting or try using calligraphy pens, contributing beautifully to the classroom community.
  • Extensions and Research: The classification tree is a dynamic tool meant to grow. Empower children to bring in new information and discoveries to expand the tree, which builds ownership and pride. Familiar examples like sunflowers and mints help link abstract classification with observable traits.
  • For the Guide: This lesson can feel daunting at first, but once you get your hands on it, it makes a lot of sense. Practice this setup beforehand.
