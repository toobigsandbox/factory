Biology // Classification

KV Families

Prepared by: Elim Lee // AMI Elementary Submission

Purpose

To transition from general plant identification to the family level in botanical classification. To help children observe subtle characteristics—like venation, petal/sepal number, and symmetry—and to connect abstract botanical taxonomy to real-world, familiar plants and fruits.

Materials List

  • Classroom Flower Specimen: A fresh flower prepared for dissection (e.g., a rose or wild flower from the Rosaceae family, or fresh radish leaves showing Cruciferae characteristics).
  • Botanical Reference Text: Plant Families by Carol Learner, kept accessible on the research shelf.
  • Tactile Dissection Kit: Magnifying lenses, dissecting needles, tweezers, black construction paper, and safety glass slides.
  • Creative follow-ups: Mortar and pestle (for pigment extraction), mesh screens (for petal paper-making), or dedicated classroom compost buckets.
Fig 1.1: Symmetrical Floral Dissection Mapping stipules & inward anthers (Rosaceae Profile)

Presentation

Show the children the book Plant Families by Carol Learner, explaining its usefulness as a reference. Have a specimen prepared that provides a clear opportunity for success in identification.

Now that we're moving to the family level, you might have explored plants independently up to this point. But when you get to identifying the family, the characteristics can be a bit harder to spot. So, we are going to talk through the details together. A great classroom resource I recommend is Plant Families by Carol Learner. When deciding which plant to study first, pick one where you can have a clear success. For example, if you’ve grown radishes, look closely at the radish leaves. You’ll find they belong to the mustard family. Or you can look at a specimen right here in the classroom. That’s what we’re going to do today.

Present the flower specimen to the children and invite them to observe it closely, fostering recall by asking them to state what they already know.

Alright, today let's look at this flower and figure out what family it’s from. First, let’s say everything you know about this flower. Remember, we already know it’s a dicot because of its leaf venation. That’s an easy way to tell. It has separate petals, so it’s an archichlamydeous. But there are many families, like Cruciferae, Rosaceae... can the name give us a clue? Let’s find out.

Carefully dissect the flower to show petals, sepals, and stamens to the children, possibly using a magnifying glass to zoom in. Encourage the children to count the parts.

Flowers in this family are radially symmetrical, meaning they’re symmetrical around a center point like a circle. They usually have five free petals and five sepals. Let’s count—how many sepals does this flower have? Five? Looks like a perfect star, doesn’t it? It seems to have more than five petals though. We’ve done rose dissections before, and the sepals give us a clue here. The stamens are numerous, and their anthers turn inward. Let’s take a closer look.

Point out the numerous stamens on the flower. Demonstrate or discuss how to extract dye from the petals, and suggest drying petals for crafts or composting.

Look at all these stamens—there are so many! If you’ve done floral dissections before, maybe with a rose, you’ve seen this kind of structure. You can also use the petals to extract dye and see what color you get. If you dry the petals, you’ll have beautiful paper decorations for the classroom. This is a great way to use dry plant material for compost too.

Point to the stipules on the leaves. Show pictures or examples of related fruits like strawberries and cherries, linking their family connections. Summarize the classification process and encourage further exploration.

See these little stipules on the leaves? That’s a helpful identifying feature too. Did you know that wild roses are in the same family as strawberries, raspberries, apples, and cherries? I didn’t know that either! And also hawthorn, plum, and cherry are part of this family. Isn’t it amazing that three different types of fruit all belong to one family? Now we know this rose belongs to the Rosaceae family and we understand its whole lineage. Scientists even classify plants further into genus and species. There’s so much fascinating stuff to learn about plants—it’s fantastic!

Pedagogical Insights & System Properties

Tagging In: At the family level, characteristics become subtler. Unlike broader classification work where children can be highly independent, family identification requires you to "tag in" with them, model your thinking out loud, and carefully interpret the details together.

Set up for Success: Always start with plants that provide a clear opportunity for success. Radish leaves (mustard family) or roses (Rosaceae family) are excellent starting points.

Surprising Diversity: Emphasize the fascinating diversity within a single plant family (e.g., the Rosaceae family includes roses, strawberries, apples, and plums).

Cross-Curricular Extensions: Hands-on activities such as floral dissection, dye extraction, and using dried petals to create classroom paper beautifully merge botanical science with art and ecological sustainability.
