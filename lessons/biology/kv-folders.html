Biology // Classification

Kingdom Vegetalia: Presentation of Folders

Prepared by: Elim Lee // AMI Elementary Submission

Purpose

To introduce Kingdom Vegetalia through the observation and classification of plants using tangible educational folders. To help children develop scientific thinking and critical observation skills by making binary decisions ("Is it this or that?") based on clear, observable plant traits.

Materials List

  • Kingdom Vegetalia Folder/Box: Color-coded forest green structural container housing sorting compartments.
  • Scientific Nomenclature Cards: Dual-layered text cards outlining characteristics, matching photo/picture cards, and circular taxonomy labels.
  • Tactile Botanical Specimens: Real specimens displaying divergent traits (e.g., woody conifer pine cones, cycads, ginkgo leaves, and herbaceous flowering samples).
  • Movable Nomenclature Tickets: Pre-labeled reference tickets indicating structural classification levels (Kingdom, Division, Class).

Presentation

Before beginning the formal lesson with the folders, take the children outside on a nature walk. Encourage them to observe what’s around them using scientific names they already know.

Before you begin this lesson, let's explore outside. I see flowers with separate petals—these are called polypetalous flowers. And look at these over here—what do you notice about their petals? Yes, those are gamopetalous flowers. Isn’t that amazing? We want to compare what we observe in nature and notice the beautiful details.

Gather the Kingdom Vegetalia materials and open the box. Sort the text cards, picture cards, and classification circles by color.

Let’s take turns reading this text card. There are four groups, so each of you can have a chance if you want. Reading aloud is optional—no pressure if you don’t want to read in front of the group. Kingdom Vegetalia means ‘to grow’ in Latin. Although animals grow too, plants are called vegetalia because they are fixed in one place and don’t have feelings or memories like animals do. Plants are unique because they make their own food.

Show the children picture cards depicting plants growing in various environments (e.g., pushing through cracks in the playground). Invite them to describe what they see.

The plant kingdom is divided into two main groups: division ‘ferogame’—plants that produce flowers—and division ‘cryptogam’—plants that don’t produce flowers. Let’s look at these groups together. Which one do you want to explore first?

Fig 1.1: Kingdom Vegetalia Material Layout (Text Card, Picture Card, and Circle Label)

Demonstrate how to organize the cards back into the folder: stacking the text cards and picture cards with the main folder card on top and then the circular label. Spread out the ferogame cards on the floor to show the pictures and text.

We usually do this work on the floor so everything spreads out nicely. Let’s start with the division ferogame because flowers are easy to observe. The name ferogame comes from Greek words meaning ‘visible marriage’ because you can see how these plants reproduce through flowers. This division includes all seed-producing plants.

Show pine cones and cycads to the children, inviting tactile exploration and storytelling to deepen interest.

Within division ferogame, there are two branches: angiosperm and gymnosperm. Angiosperm means ‘seed in a vessel’—flowers where seeds develop inside a protective vessel. Gymnosperm means ‘naked seed’—plants like pines where the seeds are exposed. We have pine cones here that you can look at to see this. Did you know that cycads have male and female plants, and ginkgo females produce smelly fruit? These little stories help you remember the plants better.

Open the Class Dicotyledonae folder. Open the subclass Archichlamydeae envelope and lay out the text card, the peony specimen card, and the corresponding circular label disc.

Within angiosperms, we discover the class of Dicotyledons—plants that split wide open showing two seed leaves. Dicots are divided into two subclasses. The first is Subclass Archichlamydeae. 'Archi-' means 'ancient' or 'first,' and 'chlamys' means 'cloak.' These are the ancient cloaks! The flowers in this group have a corolla where the petals are completely free and separate from each other, or lacking altogether. If we look at this peony, each petal stands alone. We call these flowers polypetalae.

Open the Metachlamydeae subclass envelope and place the united petal morning glory / petunia card next to the peony card for visual comparison.

The second dicot subclass is Subclass Metachlamydeae. 'Meta-' means 'recent' or 'changed.' These are the changed, recent cloaks of our plant world! In this subclass, the petals are more or less united. They are fused through part of their length into an elegant tube which surrounds the stamens and carpels, like this petunia. We call these flowers gamopetalae.

Open the Class Monocotyledonae folder and layout the subclass envelopes for Monocot Group I (Scaly Bracts) and Monocot Group II (Petaloid Sepals).

Class Monocotyledonae also divides beautifully into two subgroups! Monocot Group I plants have flowers with no floral envelopes—they are green, leathery, and covered by scaly, chaffy bracts like cattails or field grasses. They rely on the wind. But Monocot Group II has showy dresses! Their sepals are colored exactly like petals—petaloid sepals—like this tiger lily, to invite insect friends to visit.

Encourage the children’s independent exploration with the folders. Invite them to use their best handwriting to label and draw plant designs on blank circular discs, and help build out the taxonomic layout.

This whole process of classification is like a game of ‘Is it this or is it that?’ We look at characteristics and decide which group a plant belongs to. Some of you will write your subclass names on these blank circular labels using your absolute best calligraphy, and others will enjoy exploring these beautiful folders with me.

Tactile Folder Sorting Studio

Sensory simulation of the physical card sets! Tap through the taxonomy folders to inspect and read labels.

Pedagogical Insights & System Properties

Pacing and Choice: This material is designed to be simple yet deep. Allow children to explore at their own pace. This is a process meant to be done together over several lessons, not all at once. Pause and let them investigate when their curiosity is piqued.

Methodology: The classification process encourages children to make binary decisions ("Is it this or that?"). This approach supports building critical observation skills and systematic thinking.

Reading Aloud: When working with the text cards, always make reading aloud optional. This removes pressure and allows children to absorb information gradually in a supportive environment.

Organization: Teach children how to organize the cards back into the folder. This helps them learn to care for and manage the materials independently.
