Kingdom Animalia: Presentation of the Folders

Guide Name

Elim Lee

Direct Aim & Purpose

To introduce the classification of Kingdom Animalia through nested educational folders. To explore the fundamental differences between plants and animals—specifically movement, irritability, and nutrition—and to invite independent exploration of the animal phyla, classes, orders, and families.

Apparatus & Material Design

  • • Kingdom Animalia Folders: Nested folders colored from dark red (Kingdom level) down to lighter shades of red (Family level), decreasing in size. (Refer to photographic archives in KA folder_2.jpeg and schema details in Biology Material_2).
  • • Kingdom Animalia Classification Circles: Diminishing in diameter by exactly ½" at each progressive taxonomic level to sensorialize grouping limits.
  • • Taxonomic Printed Inserts: High-precision text cards, biological picture cards, and subdivision diagrams developed for key classes (e.g., Class Amphibia, Class Arachnida) and families (Family Bufonidae, Family Hylidae, Family Pelobatidae, Family Ranidae).
  • • Exploratory Supplies: Blank paper circles (plain white or with pre-printed red outer borders) and high-quality colored pencils matching the precise folder shades.

Presentation

1

Before bringing out the material, facilitate a nature walk with the children, prompting them to observe animals and insects closely. This real-world engagement helps children connect the material with their surroundings.

"Just like we did with Kingdom Vegetalia, let's take a walk outside to observe the diversity of animals. Notice the insects and how they move differently. What are the differences between the plants we studied and the animals we see? Plants can't move but make their own food, while animals can move but do not make their own food. Let's go inside and see how scientists organize all these animals."

2

Present the folders containing the Kingdom Animalia materials, setting them up in a similar way as the Kingdom Vegetalia folders. Reference the previous plant lessons to build continuity and scaffold learning.

"Here’s our Kingdom Animalia material. There are so many animals that they make up a whole kingdom! Just like with plants, we have a big circle representing the kingdom and smaller groups within it. Remember, the material here is more limited because the animal kingdom is much more vast. So, we are only focusing on what is represented here in the material, not everything that exists in the world."

3

Organize the materials on the table, pulling out the main text cards, the kingdom circle, and visual aids. Invite the children to take turns reading aloud, ensuring they know they can simply say "pass" if they prefer to listen.

"Let's look inside and see what we have here. We have text cards that explain things. Would anyone like to take a turn reading? The name Animalia comes from the Latin word 'anima,' meaning breath or soul. This implies the ability to feel and move. Plants are rooted and cannot move or feel pain or pleasure. Animals, however, are endowed with irritability, meaning they can feel pleasure and pain and respond to stimuli."

4

Relate the material to real-life examples children can understand, encouraging them to think about how animals behave, survive, and eat.

"For example, if you put your finger on a hot stove, you feel pain and pull your hand away. If you smell fresh bread, you move toward it to eat. Unlike plants that make their own food from gases, water, and minerals, animals must eat plants or other animals to survive. Through their feeding, animals reduce the number of living things and help keep the balance in nature."

5

Show the children the phyla cards and invite them to explore the boxes containing detailed information. Point out the specific groups developed within the classroom material.

"Our Kingdom Animalia material is divided into main groups called phyla, but there are many more than what we have here. Today, you can see that we have two phyla represented: Chordata and Arthropoda. You can explore these groups in the material. It’s okay if the material is limited—children are usually fascinated by facts about animals, so I encourage you to dig in."

6

Point to the blank circles and colored pencils on the shelf. Offer flexible options for exploration: independent group work or guided sessions, fostering autonomy and curiosity.

"You can explore the materials on your own as a group or we can explore together. Whether you want to use all the pieces or just some, we support your choices. We also have blank circles with borders here. If you want to create your own way of organizing what you learn, or if you want to research a phylum we don't have a folder for, you can make your own materials to add to our classroom!"

Pedagogical Insights & Guide Notes

Material Design: The folders and circles visually represent taxonomy through size and color. They progress from dark red (Kingdom) to lighter shades of red (Family), with the circles diminishing in size by 1/2" at each level to show the narrowing of the groups. (Compare with plant branches in KV subphylum V.jpeg and vertebrate templates in KA subphylum C.jpeg).

Managing Scope: Because the animal kingdom is so vast, classroom materials usually only fully develop Chordata and Arthropoda (see card layouts in KA phylum A.jpeg and KA Phylum C.jpeg). Be careful with your language: emphasize that this material only represents a part of what exists in the world.

Freedom of Choice: Reading aloud should always be optional to keep the environment inviting. Children can track progress within their Cumulative Record_2 without fear of forced performances.

Following the Child: Some years, children might dive deep into Kingdom Vegetalia; other years, they’ll be more interested in Kingdom Animalia. We follow the group's interests but make sure to present everything broadly to plant seeds of curiosity.
