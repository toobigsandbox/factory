Biology // Human Physiology

The Story of the Great River

Guide: Elim Lee // AMI Elementary Submission

Purpose

To introduce the human body as a "marvellous nation" and to present the circulatory system as the primary means of transport and communication between specialized departments.

Materials

  • The Great River Chart: (Protected Material).
  • Nomenclature: Heart, Brain, Lungs, Kidneys.
  • Metaphorical Cards: Castle, Soldiers, Windmill, Bellows, Sieve.

The Great Fable

Invite the children. Speak in a quiet, mysterious tone.
"I know of a marvellous nation where the inhabitants are more numerous than all the people on Earth combined. It is a strange country, with a strange river and strange inhabitants who never rest."
Place the Great River Chart face down, or keep it covered initially.
"The most important part of this nation is the Great River. It has branches like tributaries and channels that reach every single field and village so that no inhabitant is ever left without what they need."
Fig 1.1: The Human Body as a Marvellous Nation
Reveal the "Fortified Castle" at the top of the chart.
"Inside this nation is a strong fortified castle. The government lives here and sends commands with incredible speed and precision. Inside our own skulls, we have the Brain, which governs the whole body."
Point to the "Windmill" and the "Soldiers."
"There is a Department of Nutrition, like a windmill grinding wheat into flour. And a Department of Defence where soldiers—the white blood cells—protect the nation against invaders."
Fig 1.2: Metaphors of the specialised departments
Point to the "Sieve" (Kidneys) and the "Bellows" (Lungs).
"Every inhabitant takes what they need and puts back what they don't. The Great River carries this waste away to the Sieve to be purified. And the Bellows—the lungs—bring in the fresh air the nation needs to breathe."
"Note to the Guide: This story is as old as humanity. It relies on personification to make the complex biological functions of cells and organs accessible to the child's imagination. Always end by inviting the children to 'feel' the pulse of the Great River in their own wrists."
Elim Lee // Semester 4 Sprint // AMI Elementary
