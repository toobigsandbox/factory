Kingdom Animalia: Tracing the Genealogy of an Animal

Guide Name

Elim Lee

Direct Aim & Purpose

To trace the genealogy of a particular animal from the kingdom level down to the family level. To engage the child's reasoning mind by encouraging them to articulate the "why" behind each classification decision using observable characteristics.

Apparatus & Material Design

  • • Kingdom Animalia Classification Folders: Dark red (Kingdom) down to lighter shades of red (Family) folders containing structural text cards and key phyla subdivision guides.
  • • Classification Circles: Circular taxonomic tags with custom scientific illustrations decreasing in size by exactly ½" at each descending rank.
  • • Taxonomic Specimen: A live, touchable animal specimen inside the classroom (ideal) or a high-quality photograph, such as a tree frog (family Hylidae).
  • • Exploratory Blank Circles: Plain white cardboard circles (or pre-bordered red circles) with quality colored pencils matching the precise folder shades.

Presentation

1

Introduce the folders containing classification materials related to Kingdom Animalia, encouraging children to use their hands-on experience with these folders. Show a picture or a live specimen to the children, emphasizing the importance of observing real characteristics.

"Today we're going to explore Kingdom Animalia, similar to how we did with Kingdom Vegetalia. You'll have already spent time with the folders, exploring and deepening your understanding. Now, we’re going to engage with this material more actively by asking and answering questions together. Remember, just like with Vegetalia, the key here is to engage your reasoning mind. I will ask you 'why' questions, like 'why do you say that?' or 'why do you think that?'"

2

Direct a child to fetch the Kingdom Animalia folder to connect the specimen with its classification materials.

"Look at this picture. What do you see here? ... Yes, a tree frog! Do you think it belongs to Kingdom Vegetalia or Kingdom Animalia? ... Absolutely right! It doesn’t make its own food and it can move. Now, please bring the Kingdom Animalia folder over here."

3

Guide the children to bring over a classification circle labeled "Chordata" and place it on the workspace to visually represent the hierarchy.

"This tree frog belongs to Kingdom Animalia. Next question: does this animal have a notochord or not? ... Why do you say that? ... Because it has a backbone. Exactly! So, it belongs to Phylum Chordata. Could someone bring over that circle?"

4

Encourage the children to articulate their reasoning and then add the Vertebrata circle to the classification display.

"Now, does this tree frog have vertebrae? ... Why do you think so? ... Because it has a backbone or an inner skeleton. Perfect! That means it belongs to Subphylum Vertebrata. Great job!"

5

Have the children retrieve and place the Anura circle in the classification sequence, following your guidance.

"We know that within Vertebrata, there are five classes. Do you know which class this tree frog belongs to? ... Amphibia. Correct! Why? ... Because it’s an amphibian. Yes, and another clue is that amphibians have moist, bare skin. Now, within Amphibia, do you think it belongs to Anura, Caudata, or Gymnophiona? ... Anura. Right, because it has legs and no tail. Please bring the Anura circle."

6

Evaluate the characteristics of the tree frog with the children, guiding them to conclude it belongs to the Family Hylidae.

"Within Anura, there are several families. Let’s look at the key characteristics to find the correct family. Does it have vertical pupils? Yes. Does it have a spade? No. How about a narrow pointed head? Yes. Narrow waist? Yes. Large eardrums? No. Adhesive discs on toes and fingers? Yes. Horizontal pupils? Yes. Great! So this tree frog belongs to the Family Hylidae."

7

Foster creativity and problem solving by inviting children to personalize their learning materials, reinforcing active engagement with the classification process.

"Kingdom Animalia, Phylum Chordata, Subphylum Vertebrata, Class Amphibia, Order Anura, Family Hylidae. Amazing! Would you like to choose another animal and see how it’s classified? If there aren't circles for it, we can make them or draw them!"

Pedagogical Insights & Guide Notes

The "Why": The entire process is designed to develop a child’s ability to think through the reasons behind classification choices. Rather than memorizing static facts, always ask "why" to prompt structural and physiological analysis based on prior lessons.

Specimens: Ideal teaching involves live specimens (such as classroom terrariums housing earthworms or pond frogs) for direct observation. If using pictures, rotate them regularly for variety to show that families like Hylidae contain vast geographic varieties.

Student-Driven Creation: If circles or taxonomic materials are missing for an animal a child wants to classify, encourage them to create their own using blank circles and colored pencils. This builds fine motor control, handwriting integration, and scientific research habits documented in their Cumulative Record_3.
