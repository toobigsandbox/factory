Kingdom Animalia: Tree of Classification

Guide Name

Elim Lee

Direct Aim & Purpose

To crystallize and summarize all the classification work of the Kingdom Animalia. To build a classification tree of Kingdom Animalia from kingdom down to the family level using hands-on materials, understanding the hierarchy of phyla, subphyla, classes, orders, and families.

Apparatus & Material Design

  • • A large designated floor space or wall display area.
  • • Kingdom Animalia Classification Folders: Red folders varying in size that fit successively inside one another, progressing from dark red (Kingdom level) to lighter shades of red (Family level). Each folder displays its taxonomic name and hand-drawn characteristics.
  • • Classification Circles: Red circular cardboard cards containing hand-drawn pictures of identifying characteristics. The circles diminish by ½" in size at each progressive taxonomic step (Kingdom → Phylum → Subphylum → Class → Order → Family).
  • • Paper Branches: Sturdy brown paper branches that visually structure the tree layout, reducing by ½" in width from the central trunks down to the fine family branches.
  • • Creative Materials: Package of blank paper circles (with and without matching red borders) and high-quality colored pencils for child-led exploration.
Diagram I: Kingdom Animalia Tree Branching Layout

*Branches reduce in width by ½" at each division. Red circles diminish in diameter by ½" progressively, while colors transition from deep burgundy (Kingdom) to soft coral rose (Family).

Presentation

1

Set up a large workspace or wall area to create the classification tree. Prepare the folder with circles representing various phyla of Kingdom Animalia, placing it visibly on the shelf for easy access.

"Alright, today we’re going to explore Kingdom Animalia and build its classification tree. Just like we did with Kingdom Vegetalia, we’ll need a big space to lay everything out. Remember, you are already familiar with Kingdom Vegetalia, so you have some background before we start this."

2

Place the dark red Kingdom Animalia circle at the start of the tree on the workspace. Take out circles representing different phyla from the folder and prepare to arrange them.

"Let’s start just like with Kingdom Vegetalia. Here I have the circle for Kingdom Animalia. I’ll place it right at the beginning of our tree. Now, in Kingdom Animalia, there are many different phyla, and we have some of these in our classroom for you to study."

3

Physically place circles for worms (Annelida), Chordata, echinoderms, sponges (Porifera), protozoa, and mollusks on the tree. As each circle is placed, engage children by describing key features of each group.

"Here’s our branch—let’s put some of the phyla we know. Look, we have worms! And here’s Chordata—not ‘cord’—remember? We can talk about their characteristics as we place them: spiky skin and radial symmetry for echinoderms, porous bodies for sponges, and don’t forget the protozoa and mollusks we have in our classroom too. Let’s discuss each group as we put them on the tree."

4

Invite children to choose either Arthropoda or Chordata to explore in detail. Begin laying out the chosen branch with corresponding materials and circles.

"Some of these phyla are divided further. In our classroom, we might focus on Arthropoda or Chordata. Would you like to build out one branch fully? We’ll take one branch first, then come back to the other."

5

Encourage children to place circles representing the classes of Arthropoda they made themselves. Move attention to Chordata materials and prompt them to identify subphyla present.

"Within Arthropoda, there are many classes. Remember our research? We found five classes, but two are extinct. Scientists still count them, probably because they’re important for understanding the timeline of life. You can use your circles to fill out this part of the tree. Now, what about Chordata? What subphyla do we have here?"

6

Show the existing Amphibia circle and place it on the tree. Provide extra materials for children to create circles for other vertebrate classes like birds. Continue extending the tree from Amphibia.

"That’s right, we have vertebrates in Chordata. How many classes of vertebrates do you remember? Five! Great job! Look, there’s a little knot on the floor here — we already have Amphibia built in our materials and I have an extra circle for Birds. Would you like to make circles for the other vertebrate classes? Let’s keep building out from Amphibia."

7

Rearrange the workspace to allow room for expansion. Use brown paper to create more branches and circles. Reference previous animal studies (like the tree frog) to connect the concept of families.

"How many orders do we have in Amphibia? Three, exactly! And within those orders, we have families. Remember when we studied the tree frog? That helped us understand how scientists decide what family an animal belongs to. We have plenty of space and brown paper here, so we can keep making more branches and expand our tree bigger and bigger as we classify animals just like real scientists do."

Pedagogical Insights & Guide Notes

Material Design & Diminishing Senses: Kingdom Animalia materials intentionally progress from dark red (Kingdom) to lighter shades of red (Families). The visual and physical diminution of circles (diminishing in size by ½" at each progressive level) allows the child to subconsciously abstract and sensory-classify the hierarchical scope.

Historical Context & Scientific Integrity: Include extinct classes (specifically those within Phylum Arthropoda) directly on the tree. This bridges perfectly with their work on the Timeline of Life, validating that science is an evolving, historical record.

Limitation as an Invitation: The prepared materials explicitly limit structured classifications down to the Family level. This strategic limitation, paired with providing empty writing circles on the shelf, acts as a cosmic "call to action" inviting children to continue independent research to append their own Genus and Species cards to the display.

Prerequisites: Children must have vast experience observing classroom animals (snails, arachnids, worms, fish), working with the Body Function Material (understanding backbone structures), and completing the Kingdom Vegetalia Tree of Classification prior to this presentation.
