KV Genealogy of a Plant

Prepared by: Elim Lee // AMI Elementary Submission

Purpose

To discover the genealogy of a plant by tracing its classification from kingdom down to family. To foster critical observation skills by always identifying the reasoning (the "why") behind every classification decision using real plant specimens.

Materials List

  • Real plant specimen: A fresh, living rose flower cut from a bouquet or classroom garden, showing complete leaves, stipules, sepals, and reproductive structures.
  • Kingdom Vegetalia Classification Circles: Six concentric paper discs with distinct green-shaded borders representing taxonomic hierarchy:
    • Kingdom (Forest Green)
    • Division (Dark Sage)
    • Branch (Medium Sage)
    • Class (Light Sage)
    • Subclass (Pale Green)
    • Family (White Center with Quadrants)
  • Blank extension circles: Writable paper discs matching the taxonomic tier colors.
  • Calligraphy supplies: Matching colored pencils and fine pens for custom taxonomy labeling.
Fig 1.1: Tracing the Genealogy Circle discs sitting inside concentric arrangements (Rosaceae spec)

Presentation

Show a real rose specimen, encouraging children to observe it closely. Mention having a real flower from either a bouquet or garden for hands-on exploration.

Today, we are going to explore the genealogy of a plant. I’m going to choose the rose because you’ve already seen this material through the rose, and it’s useful for your learning and understanding. Remember, we don’t have to restrict children; we want them to explore freely. If anyone wants to create another family card or folder, I’m happy to guide you through that as well. But for now, we’ll use the rose for your comfort and familiarity.

Place the rose specimen on the table, inviting children to look at it closely. Guide the child to pick up the Kingdom Vegetalia material and place the correct circle on the working area. Model the process patiently to reinforce understanding.

The most important thing when you do this is the ‘why.’ Always ask yourself, ‘Why are you saying that? What makes you think that?’ Let’s start together. Today, we are going to discover the genealogy of this plant. First question: Do we want the Kingdom Vegetalia material or the Kingdom Animalia material? Obviously, this is Kingdom Vegetalia. Why do you think that? Because it makes its own food and it can’t move. That’s right! So, go ahead and get the Kingdom Vegetalia material.

The child selects and places the Ferro circle next to the specimen, following your guidance. Continue to prompt questions to support their reasoning.

This belongs to Kingdom Vegetalia. Now, within this kingdom, there are two divisions: Ferro-game and Cryptog-game. Which one does this belong to? Ferro? Why? Because we can see how it reproduces. Exactly! So, bring out the Ferro circle.

The child brings the Angiosperm circle and places it correctly. Encourage the child to recall prior hands-on activities, like floral dissection, to reinforce their learning.

Within Ferro gam, there are two branches: Gymnosperm and Angiosperm. Which does this belong to? Angiosperm? Why? Because the seeds are enclosed in a vessel, remember when we dissected that? Perfect! Place the Angiosperm circle here.

The child selects the Dicotyledon circle and places it down. Highlight key observable features on the real specimen, like leaf veins and flower parts, to foster critical observation.

Next, within Angiosperm, there are two classes. Which class does this belong to? Dicotyledons? Why? Because you can see the reticulate veins in the leaves and the flower parts come in fours or fives. Exactly! Bring over the Dicotyledon circle.

The child places the Archiclamer circle and then the Rose family card next to the specimen. Guide the child to count petals and flower parts (1, 2, 3, 4, 5) and observe leaf characteristics.

Within Dicotyledons, we have two subclasses: Metaclamer and Archiclamer. Which one does this rose belong to? Archiclamer? Why? Because it has poly petalis, many petals. That’s right. And within this subclass, there are several families. What family is this? The rose family! Yes! We can see radial symmetry, flower parts in fours or fives, and stipulate leaves—these are good indicators.

Point to the colored circles and pencils on the shelf, inviting children to create new material if needed. This encourages creativity and ownership of learning.

So for this specimen, we know it belongs to Kingdom Vegetalia, division Ferro, branch Angiosperm, class Dicotyledon, subclass Archiclamer, and family Rose. You can do this for any plant! If you reach a point where something isn’t in the material or is in Gymnosperm but not developed yet, don’t worry. We have circles and colored pencils on the shelf in these exact colors so you can make your own matching circles and trace the full genealogy of your specimen. Isn’t that exciting?

Pedagogical Insights & System Properties

The Power of "Why": The most crucial part of this lesson is having the child verbalize the reasoning behind each step. It is not just about sorting cards; it is about justifying the taxonomy based on observable traits.

Real Specimens: Always use actual plant specimens (like a real rose) alongside the material to ground the abstract classification hierarchy in tangible reality.

Empowering Extensions: Do not restrict children to just the pre-made materials. Ensure colored pencils and blank circles are available so children can research, create new family cards, and map out the genealogies of plants not currently in the materials.
