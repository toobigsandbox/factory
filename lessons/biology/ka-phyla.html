Kingdom Animalia: Presentation of the Phyla

Guide Name

Elim Lee

Direct Aim & Purpose

To introduce the various phyla of Kingdom Animalia (such as Porifera, Cnidaria, Annelida, Mollusca, Echinodermata, Arthropoda, and Chordata) using engaging storytelling, hands-on specimens, and creative handwork, connecting children's prior experiences to scientific classification.

Apparatus & Material Design

  • • Classification Folders: Red folders containing curated, hand-painted pictures or diagrams representing key animal phyla.
  • • Isolated Invertebrate Cards: Selected animal cards extracted from the previously worked Body Function Material (specifically focusing on invertebrates).
  • • Hands-On Specimens: Real specimens where available—either live or preserved. Recommended: natural bath sponges, real seashells, corals, local insects, garden arachnids, or a small earthworm tray.
  • • Creative Materials: Quality multi-media handwork supplies, including drawing paper, colored pencils, fine embroidery thread with needles, clay or modeling dough, and wheat paste or paper mâché supplies.

Presentation

1

Introduce the phyla with visual materials prepared. Explain the scope of the classroom materials.

"Alright, let's talk about the phyla of Kingdom Animalia. In our material, we only have two phyla that are pre-developed all the way through: Chordata, because that's where humans belong, and Arthropoda, because most of the animals in the world are arthropods. When we present an invertebrate and bring your attention to its phylum, we’re going to do that by telling a story. The story will include what the animal needs for survival, how it lives its life, where it lives, who its enemies are, and what its defenses are."

2

Pull out relevant animal cards focusing on invertebrates from the body function material. Show live or preserved specimens to the children for hands-on observation.

"Do you remember how we removed some cards from the animal shelf when we did the body function material because we only needed the vertebrates? Now, we get to pull out the invertebrates and share little stories about them. Look at this live specimen we have today. Let's observe it and notice things together. Maybe you have already had an experience with these animals outside, so we can build on that."

3

Show timeline visuals and examples of Protozoa. Demonstrate their different movements. Then move on to Porifera using sponge specimens or images.

"Let's explore some phyla! Protozoa means 'first animals.' They have classes like amoebas, ciliates, and flagellates. We can actually find them on our Timeline of Life. Then we have Porifera, which means 'pore bearing'—these are our sponges. We have the bath sponge, and even the elegant glass sponge. In Japanese culture, the glass sponge symbolizes a wedding vow because small shrimp grow inside the cavity and stay together forever."

4

Show pictures, videos, or specimens of Cnidaria and Annelida. Discuss worm anatomy, highlighting the bristles and clitellum. Bring out real earthworms if possible.

"Cnidaria are animals with stinging cells, like sea anemones, corals, and jellyfish. Then we have Annelida, meaning 'little ring'—these are segmented worms like earthworms. Earthworms have rings with bristles for traction, and a swollen part near the head where eggs are stored. Worms eat soil and are great for compost! Charles Darwin even studied them for 20 years."

5

Show specimens or images of Echinodermata and Mollusca. Explain their regeneration and defense mechanisms. Display images of octopuses and squids, pointing out their unique anatomy.

"Echinodermata means 'spiny skin'—like sea urchins, sea stars, and sea cucumbers. They can even regenerate their arms! Mollusca means 'soft body,' including snails, clams, squid, and the octopus. The octopus belongs to Cephalopoda, meaning 'head foot.' They are highly intelligent and have incredibly well-developed eyeballs."

6

Use insect and arthropod models or specimens to highlight jointed appendages. Show diagrams of vertebrate anatomy focusing on the notochord. Guide children toward follow-up projects.

"Arthropoda means 'jointed foot'—this includes insects, crustaceans, spiders, and mites. This is a massive group! Finally, Chordata means 'string,' referring to the notochord that develops into the spinal cord for vertebrates. Now that we've heard these stories, how do you want to express what you learned? You can draw, embroider, paint, build models, or even make life-size paper mâché creatures!"

Pedagogical Insights & Guide Notes

Storytelling Approach: When introducing invertebrates, it is paramount to avoid simple lists of dry definitions. Always capture the imagination through a narrative focusing on the ecological habitat, bodily survival adaptations, defensive methods, and specific enemies of the creature.

Live vs. Preserved Specimens: Real touchable assets like an authentic natural bath sponge, live garden earthworms, and detailed seashells bridge taxonomic names to physical realities, solidifying early sensory classifications.

Follow-up Handwork: Do not overlook creative expressions. Allowing children to draft scientific drawings, paint detailed biological profiles, embroider invertebrate contours, or model anatomies out of paper mâché enhances fine motor coordination and critical observation skills.

Pre-Developed Folders: Remind children that only Arthropoda and Chordata are fully pre-developed in standard shelf folders. The remaining phyla are introduced directly via these storytelling presentations specifically to ignite their own independent research, encouraging them to construct folders themselves.
