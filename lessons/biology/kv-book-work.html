Biology // Classification

Kingdom Vegetalia Book Work

Prepared by: Elim Lee // AMI Elementary Submission

Purpose

To familiarize children with using a classification resource book and dichotomous keys to systematically identify and classify plant species in a hands-on, practical manner.

Materials List

  • Kingdom Vegetalia classification resource book: A book structured with dichotomous keys designed specifically for children to systematically identify species.
  • Real plant species samples: Fresh, living leaves, flowers, or twigs, or high-quality clear pictures representing varied structural plant traits.
  • Local flora supplement: If available, a regional flora guide (e.g., California flora supplement) to contextualize and enhance the accuracy of species classification based on geographic parameters.
Fig 1.1: Standard Dichotomous Key Structure in the Reference Flora Book

Presentation

Hold up the classification resource book and show the pages where the keys are located. Open the book together with the students to look at the layout.

Today, we’re going to work with the Kingdom Vegetalia classification using this handy resource book. You’ll want to get familiar with how this book is organized so you can guide the children through keying out different species. Let’s open the book together and look at the way the keys are arranged. You might notice that the book organizes species by dichotomous keys, which means you follow a series of choices that lead you to the correct species. If you have a copy of this or a similar classification book in your classroom, now is a great time to start practicing how best to use it.

Demonstrate selecting a species sample (a leaf, flower, or picture) and show how to follow the dichotomous keys in the book step by step.

When you work with the children, the goal is for them to pick up a species and try to key it out using the book themselves. This hands-on experience is key to their learning. Remember, although scientific classification books are generally similar, each one has its own layout and nuances, so you’ll want to understand your specific resource well enough to present it clearly. Stick with the dichotomous classification as far as you can, until you reach a point where the key no longer applies. Let’s practice together by choosing a sample and using the key to identify it. I will walk you through this first, and then you can try it with your students.

Invite the participants to try the keying activity using their own books and species samples. Offer to provide feedback and support.

If you have one of these books in your classroom, I encourage you to start practicing right away. Feel free to reach out if you’d like feedback on how to use it more effectively. We’re here to support you as you help children explore plant classification. Take some time to experiment with the keys and see how the book works. This practice will make your teaching smoother and more engaging for your students.

Instructor's Aside Box

Teacher Preparation: Mastery of the specific resource book is essential. Because every classification book has its own nuances and layout, you must familiarize yourself thoroughly with the keys prior to presenting the material.

Dichotomous Keys: The core teaching point is to adhere to dichotomous classification (successive either/or choices) as much as possible until no alternatives remain. This greatly simplifies the decision-making process for the child.

Local Context: Having a local flora supplement (like the mentioned California flora supplement) is highly recommended, as it enhances accurate species identification in your specific geographic context.

Pedagogy: This work should be highly hands-on. The ultimate goal is for the children to pick up a specimen and independently key it out themselves.
